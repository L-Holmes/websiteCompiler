**"DON'T DONATE TO ME" — Version 3**

---

My gran used to save £2 coins in a tin on the kitchen windowsill.

Nothing romantic about it. She just didn't trust banks much, and she liked the weight of them.

When the tin got full she'd take it somewhere. Sometimes a day out. Sometimes something for the house. Once, memorably, a coat she'd wanted for three years.

She never once put it in a charity box in a supermarket.

Not because she was selfish. Because she understood, in the way that generation did, that you look after your own first. And that looking after your own *is* looking after the world.

I've been thinking about that tin a lot lately.

---

There's a version of generosity that's actually quite self-serving.

That sounds harsh. It isn't meant to be.

It's just that donating — to anyone, for anything — produces a genuine neurological reward. A small hit of warmth. A sense of having done something.

And that feeling is real. It counts for something.

But it can also be... convenient. A way of feeling like you've contributed without anything in your actual life having to change.

The donate button is very good at producing that feeling very cheaply.

---

Think about the last year of your life.

The moments that actually mattered. The things that shifted something.

A conversation that went somewhere unexpected.
A decision you kept putting off and finally made.
Something small that turned into something bigger.

How many of those involved the internet?

How many involved money going *away* from you rather than *towards* something?

---

Here's a statistic that doesn't get talked about much.

The average UK household is currently paying for **three subscriptions they've forgotten about.** [verify — various sources put unused subscriptions at £600+ per year]

Not cancelled. Not reconsidered. Just... quietly leaving, every month, while the direct debit sits there doing its job.

We're not bad at spending money because we're reckless.

We're bad at it because we set things up once, in a moment of enthusiasm, and then never look again.

Donations work the same way. The enthusiasm was real. The moment has passed. The money keeps going.

I'm not making an argument against generosity.

I'm making an argument for *directed* generosity. Conscious generosity. The kind where you've actually thought about where the money lands and whether it does anything when it gets there.

The tin on the windowsill.

Heavy. Intentional. Going somewhere specific.

---

So here's the ask — and it's a strange one for a YouTube video.

Don't donate to me.

Take whatever you would have spent and put it somewhere you can actually see.

Towards something in your own life. Towards someone near you. Towards the version of yourself that's been waiting for a small reason to move.

And if something happens — if it goes somewhere interesting — come back and tell me.

That's all I want from this.
