**"DON'T DONATE TO ME" — Alternative Version**

---

In 2019, a man named James quit his job to stream full time.

His community rallied. Donations poured in. At peak, he was making £2,000 a month from his viewers alone.

Two years later, the donations dried up. The channel folded. James was 34, two years out of his industry, with a gap on his CV and no savings.

His most loyal donor had given him £340 over that period.

James never knew her name.

---

There's a psychological phenomenon called the "identifiable victim effect."

It's why a photo of one child raises more money than a statistic about a million.

We're wired to respond to faces, names, stories. To people we feel we *know.*

Content creators figured this out — not cynically, necessarily. It's just what parasocial relationships do. You watch someone in your home, on your commute, during your lunch break, for years. Your brain quietly files them under "people I care about."

And then the donate button appears.

And it *feels* like helping a friend.

---

Here's what's strange though.

That same feeling — that warmth, that sense of contributing to something — is available elsewhere. Pointed at things closer to your actual life.

The friend who needs someone to show up.
The local thing that needs one more person to make it work.
The version of yourself that needs the money more than a stranger with a camera does.

None of those come with a notification that says "thanks for the support."

But they tend to matter more.

---

There's a concept in economics called "crowding out."

The theory goes: when people feel they've already done something good, they're less likely to do another good thing.

Donated to a creator this month? Slightly less likely to notice the thing closer to home that needs attention.

It's not a character flaw. It's just how moral accounting works in a brain that's trying to get through the day.

The donate button isn't neutral. It scratches an itch.

And scratched itches don't tend to itch again for a while.

---

The NHS mental health waiting list. [verify current figure]

Six weeks for a therapist on a good day.

Eleven years — on average — between someone developing symptoms and receiving any treatment at all.

Meanwhile, the global entertainment industry turns over £2.9 trillion a year.

These things aren't connected. And they kind of are.

---

I'm not going to tell you what to do with your money.

But I will say this: the research on what actually makes people happier is pretty consistent, and "donating to content creators" doesn't feature anywhere in it.

Experiences do. Relationships do. Feeling like you're moving towards something does.

£5. £10. £20 a month.

That's a book. A train ticket somewhere new. Half a therapy session. Entry to the thing you've been meaning to try.

It's not nothing.

---

So.

Don't donate to me.

Not because I don't appreciate it — I do, genuinely.

But because I'd rather you came back in six months and told me you spent it on something that actually changed something.

That's a better ending to the story.
