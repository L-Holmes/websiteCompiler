




### Intro / Hook

I wanted to see if you could predict happiness with just one number.

So I grabbed happiness scores for European countries…  
and started plotting them against everything I could find.

Income. Exercise. Time spent with friends. Even weird things like plastic use and vaping prevalence.

And I didn’t know what I’d find.

But pretty quickly, patterns started to emerge.

And some countries surprised me.  

This is what actually predicts happiness—and what doesn’t.


### First Discovery: Income

The first thing I tried was obvious: income per capita.

[INSERT GRAPH: happiness vs income]

At first glance, it’s clear.  
Richer countries are happier.  

But it’s not perfect.  
Some countries are richer than they “should” be based on happiness,  
and others are punching above their weight.  

Already, just one graph tells a story—but also raises more questions.


### Outliers Start to Tell a Story

Next, I zoomed in on the outliers.

[ZOOM GRAPH: highlight overperforming and underperforming countries]

Why is Slovenia happier than its income predicts?  
Why does France lag despite being wealthy?  

This is where the data gets interesting.  
Because the outliers hint at other factors shaping happiness beyond just money.


### Testing Exercise

So I plotted exercise—average hours per week or percent active population—against happiness.

[INSERT GRAPH: exercise vs happiness]

And… okay. There is a trend.  
Countries where people exercise more tend to be happier.  

But the correlation is weaker.  
Exercise alone doesn’t explain the outliers.  

Still, for the countries that were doing surprisingly well or poorly, exercise sometimes nudged the story in the right direction.


### Adding Social Factors

Next: social support.  
Time spent with friends, family, or community engagement.  

[INSERT GRAPH: social support vs happiness]

Now the story starts to make more sense.  
Slovenia, for example, ranks high in social connectivity—this helps explain why it overperforms.  
And some rich countries that lag have weaker social ties.  

This is where human stories start to emerge:  
- the communal culture of the Nordic countries  
- Mediterranean countries with strong family networks  
These aren’t just numbers—they’re lived experiences.


### Modern Lifestyle Checks

Then I tried more unusual variables:  
- plastic consumption per capita  
- vaping prevalence  
- other small lifestyle indicators  

[INSERT GRAPH: modern factors vs happiness]

Nothing.  
Almost zero correlation.  

It’s actually useful to see which factors *don’t* matter.  
Because when we see the noise, the real signals—income, exercise, social connection—stand out even more.


### Adjusting for Correlation

Here’s the tricky part: some factors overlap.  

Richer countries also tend to exercise more, and have higher social support measures.  

So I adjusted for those correlations—basically asking:  
> “If two countries have the same income, does exercise or social support still matter?”

After controlling for income:

- Exercise has some effect, but smaller than it looks.  
- Social support still nudges a few countries up or down.  
- Money is still the dominant predictor, but not the *whole story*.


### The Emerging Pattern

So here’s the takeaway:

1. **Income** is the strongest single predictor.  
2. **Social support** and **exercise** tweak the outcomes, especially for outliers.  
3. Most “modern lifestyle factors”—plastic use, vaping, trendy habits—don’t predict happiness at all.  
4. The outliers are what make the story human and memorable:  
   - Slovenia punching above its weight  
   - France underperforming despite wealth  

It’s not just a chart.  
It’s a window into how culture, society, and resources interact to shape real lives.


### Ending / Reflection

So can you predict happiness with a single number?  

Sort of.  
Money gets you most of the way there.  

But if you want to understand the *why* behind the data,  
you need to look at the stories, the culture, the people,  
and the countries that break the rules.

And that’s where the maps stop being boring numbers…  
and start being stories you actually remember.
