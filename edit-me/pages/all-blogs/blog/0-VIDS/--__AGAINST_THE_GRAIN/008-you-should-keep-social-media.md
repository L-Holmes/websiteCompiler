
You should keep social media 

**[HOOK — 0:00–0:20]**

My grandparents had the TV on all day.

We'd go to the market, the bowling green, even litter picking —
genuinely the highlight —

but when it rained? Back inside. Same four walls. Same routine.

But.. here's the thing.. 
Somewhere in the next town, a brand new *dinosaur* crazy golf had just opened.
And not just that, there was an ice hockey show at the arena — which for our North American viewers,
is somehow an exotic rarity here in the UK.

But yeah, grandad Archibald and and grandma Doris never knew. They simply had no way to find out.

*Those memories never got made.*

And that's the most underrated argument for keeping social media —
the things you never knew you missed.

---

**[THE ANCESTORS — 0:20–0:52]**

Because we genuinely weren't built to live like this.

Our ancestors were never alone in a dark room eating a Tesco meal deal.

They lived in clans. Thirty, fifty, a hundred people —
everyone knew everyone. Always outside. Always together.

And here's a fact you can tell people tonight —

rooms indoors are roughly a *hundred* times darker than outside.[1] 

Your eyes — the only sense that's literally directly plugged into your brain —
are sat in near-darkness, all day, every day.
It's just they adapt. so you don't realise it.  

It may feel fine, but really it's not. 
That's your brain running on emergency lighting all the time.. and wondering why you feel terrible.

We lost the clan. We lost the outdoors.

and well...., Social media _isn't_ a replacement —

but right now, 
It at least provides something to help with modern life. 
It gives that boost when we're not getting what we need to be happy. 

---

**[RETENTION LINE — 0:52–0:55]**

But that's not the only thing with social media. It goes even deeper: 


---

**[THE LEARNING — 0:55–1:28]**

Think about how humans actually learn best.

Again, its much again like our ancestors, it's. 
Someone showing you. Doing it. Quick, visual, right in front of you.

They've done studies with apes-- they do the same thing. You show a toy snake, and a human jumps and looks terrified-- the apes will stay away, and they visually teach their children to stay away. 
It evolved for safety. 
We have that same wiring. 

And when you're at home, at your desk, what's the closest thing to that? 
It may be hard for some to admit but... 
It's TikTok. It's YouTube.
You see others showing you, you hear their voices, you feel engaged. 

But before those existed? You either couldn't access it —
or you'd pay someone a fortune
who'd immediately upsell you into a ten-week course
on how to put a picture frame up.

But Now? you've got the best teachers alive in your pocket. Free. Right now.

Creators like the Huberman Lab taught me how to actually take care of my body.
The Gottmans are keeping my relationship together and a making me into a better partner.
Tech creators pointed me straight toward my dream job.

Without social media I don't find any of that.

I'd still be on Wookiepedia at 2am —
a certified expert in Luke and Leia's deeply strange romantic history.
Yeah.. not ideal, and not useful to my life. 

---

**[RETENTION LINE — 1:28–1:32]**

But what happened when I tried to delete it all? When I first realised and tried to step away from all the socials? 

*that's where it gets interesting.*

---

**[THE DELETE — 1:32–2:02]**

I went full anti-tech.

Blocked YouTube. Deleted everything. The whole thing.

Week one — felt great. Righteous, even. Sure it was a struggle feeling a bit bored. But that wasn't the bad thing.. 

You see, then, the loneliness hit.

Living alone. Eating alone. No inspiration.
I couldn't learn in a way that actually *moved* me into action.

So with great shame — and I say shame, but I genuinely shouldn't have —
I redownloaded YouTube and Spotify.

Haven't looked back since.

Because here's what nobody in those "delete everything" videos admits —

your brain doesn't just adapt to having no social media entertainment.

It just *replaces* it.

Subconsiously- without you realising it. 

You'd inevitably find that you've replaced it with TV. Netflix. Gaming.

All of those are Arguably worse — pure passive consumption with nothing useful on the other side.
All mercilessly trying to steal and keep the attention of the general population and along with them you...

Your brain will always find the easiest dopamine path.

That's not a personal failing. That's just biology.

---

**[RETENTION LINE — 2:02–2:06]**

And so, if you're going to eventually end up on some screen, at least choose the one that has potential to learn and excite. 
So that's what I did. 
And the next thing I realised completely changed how I use it.

---

**[THE CELEBRITIES — 2:06–2:25]**

I was thinking about all the celebrities who had dramatically exited social media..

Most of them came back.

Because it's how the modern world works.
It's how you reach people. Build things. Promote businesses. 

Quitting has a cost nobody really talks about.

And we never hear from the people who missed out by not using it —
because they don't know what they missed.

Just like my grandparents.

---

**[THE PROBLEM — 2:25–2:52]**

Here's the thing though.

Five minutes on certain Reddit communities and I'm already having a mind melting anxiety attack.

So I treat myself like a patient.

One who knows exactly what they can't be near.

No comparison content. No outrage. No Snapchat — just force people onto WhatsApp.
Sounds harsh. But it works. And it keeps us all sane. 

The problem was never the platform.

The problem is addiction.
The problem is the *content.*

---

**[RETENTION LINE — 2:52–2:56]**

So here's what I'd actually do instead of deleting everything.

---

**[THE STRATEGY — 2:56–3:35]**

Don't delete. *Edit.*

Remove the ones that make you feel worse about yourself.
Keep the ones that make you better.

Use it like a tool —
entertainment when you're genuinely down and alone,
and a way to learn things you'd never otherwise sit down and study.

Sure — 95% of what I've ever watched was probably wasted.

But that 5%.

That 5% has been genuinely pivotal.

I just had to slowly, honestly learn to find more of it.

Set limits. Be strict. Know your triggers.

And maybe — just maybe — don't use it to admire other people's lives.

Use it to build your own.

---

**[OUTRO — 3:35–4:10]**

Maybe one day you'll tell someone about the business you started.

The hike where you met your partner.

The thing you built from nothing.

You won't remember exactly what led you there —

it'll just feel like something you came up with.

Because that's the beauty of it.

Social media doesn't replace your thinking.

It helps you find what you always already knew, deep down.

So don't admire other people's lives through it.

Use it to build your own.

That version of your life is still out there.

*Go find it.*
