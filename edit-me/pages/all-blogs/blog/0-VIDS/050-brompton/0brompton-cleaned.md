
Intro (The Hook)

Two thousand years ago, the Romans built the fastest delivery system on Earth.

No, they didn't have folding bikes.

They did it by swapping transports. Ride hard. Stop. New horse. Keep going. Over and over.

The system was called the Cursus Publicus. And it was absurdly efficient.

If everything worked.

It got me thinking… in the modern day, without a car, what is the equivalent of that?
The Modern Pitch (The Discovery)

For me, the realization hit when I was staring down my own commute.

Driving was draining my soul, but taking the train had a fatal flaw: the station was too far to walk to at both ends.

Then it clicked. I needed to build my own Cursus Publicus. Just with slightly less impressive horses.

What if I just swapped transports?

Bike to the station.
Train into the city.
Bike the last bit.

On paper, it was the perfect hack. And once I had the idea, the logic was undeniable—especially when you look at what a standard commute actually takes from you.

Here’s the bit no one talks about properly: driving doesn’t just cost money. It costs time you can’t use. You’re locked in. Focused. Slightly stressed.

On a train? Different story. You can work. Watch something. Switch off. That middle chunk of your journey becomes actual life. Do that every day and you’re not saving minutes. You’re gaining days back every year.

Then there’s the cost. A car is easily £3,600 a year, and fuel alone is ridiculous.

A second-hand Brompton? About £950. Add trains, and it's still usually cheaper overall.

My pitch to myself was simple: Spend less. Gain time. And the absolute best tool to make this personal transport network work? A Brompton. It doesn't just fold "small." It fits under a desk, goes in a car boot, and slides onto a train without a fight. It removes the friction from the transitions.

Which is the whole game.
The Turn (The Catch)

It’s the exact same idea as the Romans.

But there’s a reason most people weren’t using the Cursus Publicus.

It only worked because the routes were maintained, the stations were reliable, and everything was predictable. If one part failed, the whole system slowed down.

Even in Victorian Britain, people tried this. Carriage to the station. Train. Another carriage at the other end. And they ran into the exact same issue:

Miss one link… and the whole journey falls apart.

Now apply that to today. Trains delayed. Roads not built for cycling. Weather doing whatever it wants.

Real life isn’t clean. You’re tired. It’s raining. You’re running late. Now instead of one decision—drive or not—you’ve got a chain: cycle, fold, train, unfold, cycle again. 

Each step is small. Together? It's friction.

---

## **The Illusion of Flexibility**

A car is inefficient… but brutally flexible. Leave whenever. Change plans instantly. Carry anything.

This system? It’s efficient—but conditional. 

Once you've committed to bike-plus-train, you're committed. No nipping back if you forgot something. No flexibility if you feel rough halfway through the day.

But let's play devil's advocate. Let's assume you have great cycle paths, missing trains is an afterthought, and the time and cost savings still entice you.

That is exactly the position I was in. It's why I bought my Brompton.

Do you know how many times I’ve used it alone?

Basically zero.

Not because the bike doesn’t work. Because I don’t.

---

## **The Reality of the Road**

All it takes is one close miss with a car. 

Not dramatic. Not cinematic. Just… a normal Tuesday. The driver didn’t look. I didn’t trust the road enough. Close enough.

And that’s roughly the moment I realised this *brilliant*, beautifully engineered folding bike might be completely useless to me.

Cycling sounds great until you’re dodging parked cars, squeezed by traffic, soaked in the rain, and trying not to die on a roundabout. 

If I need to be somewhere at 7 AM, am I doing a 1-hour drive? Or a **2.5-hour** mess involving trains, cycling, and stress?

---

## **The Tethered Goat (Social Friction)**

"Alright," you say. "What about short trips? Shops. Gym. The pub."

Here’s the problem with a Brompton: you can’t just *leave* it. 

Even with the best lock in the world, leaving a £1,000 folding bike outside a shop in 2026 is essentially a charitable donation to the local bolt-cutter enthusiast. 

So, you bring it inside. That’s the "ingenious" part, right? No locks, no theft.

Wrong. The British high street wasn't designed for "ingenious folding solutions." 

Try taking a folded Brompton into a quiet post office. Or a chemist. You aren't "a person with a bike"—you are a "problem with wheels." You’re the guy bumping into the shampoo display with a wet, heavy lump of steel. You’re faffing with latches while a queue of people stares at the back of your head.

Imagine you’re in a crowded pub in Glasgow. You’ve had a brilliant evening, the atmosphere is electric, and everyone decides to move to the next spot. 

Suddenly, you aren't a friend; you’re the guy with the 12kg anchor. You’re unfolding your life in the middle of a dark, sticky doorway while your mates jump in an Uber and disappear into the night. 

You aren't free. You are a **tethered goat**. You are constantly negotiating your space. 

And honestly? I’m embarrassed to ride it. People will say, “Mate, no one cares.” They’re wrong. Some people absolutely do care, and crucially—*you don’t know which one you’re about to pass*. 

The design is perfect, but the world is built of narrow aisles and judgmental silence.

---

## **The Real Test**

So, here's the actual test. 

Write down your five most common journeys. Not hypothetical ones—real ones. The badminton on Tuesday. The family visits. The supermarket run.

Now ask, honestly: does a Brompton help with any of these, specifically? Not in theory. On the actual roads, at the actual times, in the actual weather.

For each one where the answer is yes—one point.

**Two or more:** Buy it. Your life has the right shape for this bike. Unbeatable.
**One or zero:** It'll be in your hallway within a month. Sell it to someone whose life does fit.

Most people aren’t in that situation. They’re close. But not quite. And “not quite” is enough to break it.

---

## **Conclusion (The Bigger Truth)**

The problem isn’t the bike. It’s everything around it. Roads built for cars. Patchy public transport. Social pressure that sounds small but isn’t. 

If I lived somewhere quieter, flatter, and more cycle-friendly, I’d use it all the time. 

I’ll sit there thinking, *“Ooh… this would be perfect for the Brompton.”* And then I still won’t use it. Because I know I won’t enjoy the ride there.

So I listed it. Took the photos. Wrote the description. 

A tool is only useful if you actually use it. 

The Romans figured this out 2,000 years ago. Switching transport is powerful. It’s how you move efficiently. But it only works when the world around you is stable. 

The Brompton isn’t just a bike. It’s a system. And if your life doesn’t perfectly fit that system, it doesn’t matter how clever it is. It won’t save you time. It won’t save you money. It’ll just sit there, waiting for a version of your life that doesn’t quite exist.

The machine is a masterpiece. I just don't know if the world is ready for it. Yet.
