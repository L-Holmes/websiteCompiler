

---

## **Title:**

**“I bought the perfect bike. Then I stopped leaving the house.”**

---

## **Intro (hard hook, immediate story)**

I’ve done this more than once.

Keys in hand. Shoes on.
Bag packed.

Standing at the door thinking:

“Right — I’ll take the Brompton.”

…

And then just… not going.

Not delayed. Not reconsidering.

Just — *nah*.

Sit back down. Drive instead.

---

## **Set-up (mystery)**

Which is weird.

Because this bike is *perfect*.

Genuinely.

If you designed the ideal transport system from scratch —
you’d probably end up with something like this.

---

## **Story 1 — “the perfect use case”**

My friend Sarah lives just far enough away to be annoying.

Too far to cycle normally.
Too awkward by train alone.
Driving’s fine… until traffic isn’t.

The Brompton should be perfect:

Cycle to the station
Train
Cycle the last bit

Door to door. Smooth.

Faster, cheaper, less stress.

I’ve thought about doing that trip… loads of times.

Never done it.

---

## **Why it *should* work (fast, grounded)**

Because technically, it solves everything.

It folds tiny — properly tiny.
You don’t argue with train staff.
You don’t need a bike rack.
You don’t worry about parking.

It turns “that’s awkward” into “that’s doable”.

---

## **Story 2 — “the almost trip”**

I actually tried once.

Planned it. Properly.

Checked trains. Packed light.
Even folded the bike *in the house* like I was about to leave.

And then I thought about the middle bit.

Cycling through town.
Traffic. People. Tight roads.

And more than that—

Just… being seen on it.

Unfolding it at the station.
People clocking it.
That tiny moment where you feel like a bit of a knob.

So I didn’t go.

---

## **Turn (zoom out slightly)**

And this is the bit no one tells you about these things:

The limiting factor isn’t the engineering.

It’s you.

---

## **Story 3 — “the shop test”**

Alright, simple one.

Cycle to the shop.

Easy.

Except now you’ve got the bike.

Do you bring it in?

Some places are fine.
Some places absolutely aren’t.

You get that look.
Like you’ve brought something slightly unreasonable into a normal space.

So you leave it outside?

Cool. £1,000 bike. Gone in 30 seconds if you’re unlucky.

So now even a *5 minute trip* has friction.

---

## **Money + time (woven into story, not lecture)**

And it’s annoying, because the numbers are ridiculous.

Driving costs me about £3,600 a year.

Fuel alone is stupid.

The bike? Under a grand.

Trains, even added in — still often cheaper overall.

And time-wise?

Driving is dead time.

Train time is usable.
You can actually *live* in it.

So on paper, this thing is saving money *and* giving me time back.

---

## **Story 4 — “the reality check”**

But here’s what actually happens.

It’s raining.

You’ve got somewhere to be.
You’re already a bit tired.

Option A:

Warm car. Leave whenever. Done.

Option B:

Cycle in the rain
Fold bike
Train
Unfold
Cycle again

And *hope* nothing goes wrong.

You don’t pick B.

Not consistently.

---

## **Cycling reality (grounded, blunt)**

And this isn’t some abstract fear.

Cycling in the UK is… not great.

You’re negotiating with:

* drivers who aren’t looking
* roads that weren’t built for you
* weather that’s actively against you

It’s fine… until it isn’t.

And when it isn’t, it sticks with you.

---

## **Emotional core (clean, honest)**

The real reason though?

It’s simpler than all of that.

I don’t like how it feels.

Not riding it — that’s fine.

Everything around it.

The attention.
The slight awkwardness.
The constant low-level friction.

Individually, all small.

Together?

Enough to stop me leaving the house.

---

## **Contrast (what *does* work)**

And here’s the irony.

A normal bike?

I’ll use that.

No folding. No weirdness.
Lock it up. Walk away.

A car?

Obviously easy.

It’s specifically this “clever middle ground”…

…that I just don’t step into.

---

## **The trap (insight)**

Because the Brompton doesn’t fail dramatically.

It fails quietly.

You just… don’t use it.

You keep thinking you will.

You imagine the perfect trips.

You justify it.

And then weeks go by.

Nothing.

---

## **Final story beat (decision)**

I actually took photos to sell it.

Proper listing. Wrote it all out.

Then stopped halfway through and thought—

“This is such a good idea though…”

Closed the app.

Didn’t use the bike.

Opened it again a week later.

Finished the listing.

---

## **Ending (tight, memorable)**

It’s one of the smartest designs I’ve ever owned.

It genuinely solves real problems.

But it doesn’t solve *me*.

And if a solution only works in theory…

…it’s not a solution.
