## **1. The Roman “multimodal commute” (cleanest parallel)**

Most people picture Romans just… walking or riding horses.

Not quite.

The Roman Empire had a system called the **Cursus Publicus** — basically an official transport network.

And it worked like this:

* Ride a horse →
* reach a station →
* switch to a fresh horse or carriage →
* continue

Over and over.

Sound familiar?

It’s basically:

**bike → train → bike**

---

### **Why this works for your video**

* It’s recognisable (Roman Empire = instant interest)
* It directly mirrors your “switching modes” idea
* It lets you land a key insight:

> Even 2,000 years ago… the fastest way to travel wasn’t one method — it was *switching between them*.

---

### **The twist (important for your argument)**

It only worked because:

* stations were reliable
* routes were maintained
* the system was *predictable*

For normal people?

It wasn’t.

Outside that system, travel got messy fast.

👉 That maps perfectly to your point:
**Multimodal systems are powerful — but fragile.**

---

## **2. Victorian rail + carriage (more relatable, more British)**

In the 1800s, trains exploded across Britain.

But here’s the problem:

They only got you to the station.

So people did this:

* Horse carriage to station
* Train
* Another carriage at the other end

A full *chain* journey.

---

### **Why this is good**

It’s basically the *original version* of:

**bike → train → bike**

And people *loved* it…

Until it broke.

* carriage late → miss train
* train delayed → stranded
* costs stack up

Same pattern.

---

### **Useful line for your script**

> “Even in Victorian Britain, people were already doing this — stitching together journeys from multiple modes.
> And they ran into the exact same problem: it only works when everything lines up.”

## **3. Genghis Khan and the relay system (fast, dramatic)**

This one’s more dramatic.

The Mongol Empire had relay stations across huge distances.

Riders would:

* sprint between stations
* swap horses instantly
* continue at full speed

It made them absurdly fast.

---

### **Why it works**

It’s the *extreme version* of your idea:

Switching transport = speed.

---

### **The hook angle**

> “The fastest empire in history didn’t rely on one horse…
> it relied on switching horses constantly.”

---

### **Tie-back**

Again:

* worked because system = reliable
* average person = couldn’t use it

Same story.
