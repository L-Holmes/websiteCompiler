
## **"This 2,000-year-old idea could fix your commute. Don't use it."**

---

## **Intro — Hook**

Two thousand years ago, the Romans built the fastest delivery system on Earth.

No, they didn't have folding bikes. Or even bikes, for that matter.

It wasn't faster horses, either.

It was *swapping* them.

Ride hard. Stop. New horse. Keep going.

Over and over.

The system was called the Cursus Publicus.

And it was absurdly efficient.

If everything worked.

---

It got me thinking — in the modern day, without a car, what's the equivalent of that?

Obviously, if you're made of money, taxis are fastest. But let's assume no cars at all.

---

## **Snap to Present**

We're still doing the exact same thing.

Just with slightly less impressive horses.

Or — not quite as impressive as a Roman stallion — but something that gets me from A to B in one satisfying click.

---

## **The Setup — Modern Multi-Modal**

Bike to a station.

Train into a city.

Bike the last bit.

Switching modes.

On paper, it's perfect.

Faster than driving.
Cheaper than driving.
And — crucially — it gives you your time back.

---

And the best tool for it?

A Brompton.

It folds properly small. Not "folding bike small" — *fits under a desk, goes in a car boot, slides onto a train without a fight* small.

Which means you can actually do the switching:

Bike → train → bike
Bike → bus → bike
Bike → mate picks you up → done

A normal bike can't do that.

---

## **Time**

Here's the bit no one talks about properly:

Driving doesn't just cost money. It costs *time you can't use*.

You're locked in. Focused. Slightly stressed.

Train?

Different story.

You can work. Watch something. Switch off.

That middle chunk of your journey becomes actual life.

Do that every day and you're not saving minutes.

You're gaining **days** back every year.

---

## **Money**

Then there's cost.

Car: around £3,600 a year, easily. *[show calculations on screen]*

Fuel alone is ridiculous. And the initial cost of an electric car is even steeper, at least right now.

A second-hand Brompton? Around £950.

Add trains — still often cheaper overall.

So the pitch is simple:

**Spend less. Gain time.**

Sounds like a no-brainer.

---

## **Callback to History**

It's the same idea as the Romans.

Don't rely on one method.

Switch between them.

That's how you go faster.

---

## **The Turn — Why the Roman System Actually Worked**

But here's the thing.

There's a reason most people weren't using the Cursus Publicus.

It only worked because:

- the routes were maintained
- the stations were reliable
- everything was predictable

If one part failed…

The whole system slowed down.

---

## **Bring It to Modern Reality**

Now apply that to today.

Trains delayed.

Roads not built for cycling.

Weather doing whatever it wants.

Your "perfect system" starts to wobble.

And when one part wobbles — you feel it immediately.

---

Even in Victorian Britain, people were doing this. Carriage to the station. Train. Another carriage at the other end.

Same idea. Same problem.

Miss one link…

…and the whole journey falls apart.

---

## **Modern Friction**

Because real life isn't clean.

You're tired.
It's raining.
You're running late.

Now instead of one decision — drive or not — you've got a chain:

- Cycle
- Fold
- Train
- Unfold
- Cycle again

Each step small.

Together?

Friction.

---

## **The Car Problem**

A car is inefficient.

But brutally flexible.

Leave whenever. Change plans instantly. Carry anything.

This system?

It's efficient — but conditional.

Once you've committed to bike-plus-train, you're committed. No nipping back if you forgot something. No flexibility if you feel rough halfway through. If you have any kind of health condition that flares unpredictably — IBS, migraines, anything — a journey that relies on cycling at both ends is suddenly a source of anxiety rather than freedom.

---

## **Where It Does Work**

And to be fair:

If your route is a short cycle, reliable train, short cycle — this is unbeatable. Genuinely.

Faster than driving. Cheaper. Less stressful.

Here's the actual test.

Write down your five most common journeys. Not hypothetical ones — real ones. The badminton on Tuesday. The family visits. The supermarket run.

Now ask, honestly: does a Brompton help with any of these, specifically? Not in theory. On the actual roads, at the actual times, in the actual weather.

For each one where the answer is yes — one point.

Two or more: buy it. Your life has the right shape for this.

One or zero: it'll be in your hallway within a month.

---

## **Most People Are Close. But Not Quite.**

Most people aren't in that situation.

They're close.

But "not quite" is enough to break it.

---

## **Devil's Advocate — Why I Bought One**

But let's go back to being devil's advocate.

Let's assume great cycle paths. Reliable trains. The time and cost savings are real and they're tempting you.

That was exactly my position.

So I bought one.

---

## **So Why Don't I Use It?**

Because none of that matters…

…if you don't actually use it.

I've owned mine for ages.

Do you know how many times I've used it alone?

Basically zero.

Not because it doesn't work.

Because I don't.

---

## **The Moment It Changed**

All it takes is one close miss with a car.

Not dramatic. Not cinematic. Just… a normal Tuesday.

Driver didn't look. I didn't trust the road enough.

Close enough.

And that's roughly the moment I realised:

This *brilliant*, clever, beautifully engineered folding bike…

…might be completely useless to me.

---

## **Embarrassment**

I'm just… embarrassed to ride it.

Not rational. Not logical.

But real.

You feel people looking. You imagine someone laughing. You can't quite shake it.

And people will say: "Mate, no one cares."

They're wrong.

Some people don't care. Some people absolutely do.

And crucially — *you don't know which one you're about to pass*.

That's enough.

---

## **The Roads**

Then there's the actual roads.

Cycling sounds great until you're dodging parked cars, getting squeezed by traffic, soaked in the rain, and trying not to die on a roundabout.

If I need to be somewhere at seven — am I doing a one-hour drive? Or a two-and-a-half-hour mess involving trains, cycling, and stress?

---

## **The Tethered Goat**

Here's the thing no one tells you.

You can't just *leave* it.

Leaving a £1,000 folding bike outside a shop in 2026 is essentially a charitable donation to the local bolt-cutter enthusiast. So you bring it inside.

That's the ingenious bit, right?

Wrong.

Try taking a folded Brompton into a post office. Or a chemist. You aren't "a person with a bike" — you are a *problem with wheels.* You're bumping into the shampoo display, faffing with latches while a queue of people stares at the back of your head, apologising for twelve kilograms of wet steel.

I once took it on a night out in Glasgow. The logical plan: train, cycle to the pub, cycle to the hotel.

I spent the entire evening being *The Bike Guy.*

Everyone decides to move on to the next spot. And you're not a friend anymore — you're The Problem. You're the one in the sticky doorway, struggling with a latch, while your mates disappear into the night.

I couldn't stay for the last round. I had to navigate a city I didn't know on tiny wheels while everyone else hopped in an Uber.

You aren't free.

You're a **tethered goat.**

The design is perfect. But the world is built of narrow aisles and judgmental silence.

---

## **"Just Use It Locally" — Kill the Counter**

Alright — what about short trips? Shops. Gym. Badminton.

Same problem. You can't bring it inside most places. Shops don't want it. Staff give you looks. You feel like a nuisance.

So you lock it outside? Now you've got a £1,000 bike sitting there.

---

## **The Awkward Truth**

So in reality, it's only actually useful for:

- Train → bike
- Bus → bike
- Getting picked up by someone without a bike rack

That's it.

Everything else? A normal bike is easier. Or a car is.

---

## **The Worst Bit**

And here's what kills me.

I'll be sat there thinking: *"Ooh… this would be perfect for the Brompton."*

And then…

…I still won't use it.

Because I know I won't enjoy the ride there.

---

## **Credibility — I've Cycled Loads**

And before you say it —

I've cycled loads.

Commuted daily. Grew up riding everywhere. Crashed, seen crashes, dealt with all of it. *(Mention the cracked helmet.)*

This isn't fear of cycling.

It's realism about where I live.

---

## **The Bigger Truth**

The problem isn't the bike.

It's everything around it.

Roads built for cars. Public transport that's patchy. Social pressure that sounds small — but isn't.

If I lived somewhere quieter… flatter… more cycle-friendly…

I'd use it all the time.

---

## **The Decision**

But I don't.

So I listed it.

Took the photos. Wrote the description.

Because the truth is:

A tool is only useful…

…if you actually use it.

---

## **Ending — Loop Back**

The Romans figured this out 2,000 years ago.

Switching transport is powerful. It's how you go faster. It's how you move efficiently.

But it only works when the world around you is stable.

And that's the problem.

Because the Brompton isn't just a bike.

It's a system.

And if your life doesn't perfectly fit that system — it doesn't matter how clever it is.

It won't save you time.
It won't save you money.

It'll just sit there…

…waiting for a version of your life that doesn't quite exist.

---

**The machine is a masterpiece.**

**I just don't know if the world is ready for it yet.**
