## **Title:**

**“This bike can give you your life back. Don’t buy one.”**

---

## **Intro (hook — story, not personal insecurity)**

There’s a very specific kind of commute that breaks people.

Not the long one.
Not the expensive one.

The *awkward* one.

The one that should take 40 minutes…
…but somehow takes an hour and a half.

Car → traffic → parking
Train → walking → waiting

Just friction. Everywhere.

So people try to fix it.

And every now and then…

They find something that *actually works*.

---

## **Set-up (promise)**

This is one of those things.

A folding bike that:

* cuts your commute time
* saves you thousands
* and fits basically anywhere

It’s one of the smartest transport ideas I’ve seen.

And you still probably shouldn’t buy it.

---

## **The “perfect system” (paint the dream)**

Imagine this:

You leave your house.

Cycle 10 minutes — easy, no traffic.
Fold the bike. Walk straight onto a train.
Sit down. Laptop out. Coffee.

That’s 30 minutes of *actual life*.

Get off. Unfold.
Cycle the last bit.

Door to door.

No parking.
No traffic.
No wasted time.

---

## **Time (core idea, sharp)**

Because this is the real trick:

Driving *looks* fast.

But it steals your time.

You’re busy the whole way.
Mentally occupied. Slightly stressed.

Train + bike?

Part of that journey becomes usable.

Emails. Videos. Thinking. Nothing.

Over a year, that’s not minutes.

That’s **days** of your life back.

---

## **Money (quick, brutal contrast)**

Then there’s cost.

Typical car:

* ~£3,600 a year all in

Fuel alone eats you alive.

This?

~£1,000 upfront.

Add trains — still often cheaper overall.

So you’ve got:

Less money spent.
More time gained.

That’s rare.

---

## **So why doesn’t everyone do this? (turn)**

Because the system only works…

If *every step* works.

---

## **Story 1 — “the broken chain”**

Let’s say one part fails.

Train delayed.
Weather turns.
Road’s worse than expected.

Now your smooth system becomes:

Waiting. Rerouting. Standing in the rain with a folded bike.

A car absorbs that.

This doesn’t.

---

## **The hidden requirement (insight)**

This setup demands consistency.

* consistent trains
* decent cycling routes
* predictable journeys

If any of those wobble…

…the whole thing feels fragile.

---

## **Story 2 — “the UK problem”**

In theory, cities are perfect for this.

In reality:

* roads are built for cars
* cycle routes randomly disappear
* public transport is… unreliable

So instead of a smooth chain, you get gaps.

And you feel every one of them.

---

## **Convenience gap (fast, observational)**

A car is blunt, but reliable:

Leave when you want.
Go where you want.
Carry anything.

The Brompton system is clever…

…but it’s conditional.

It works brilliantly —
*when everything lines up*.

---

## **Story 3 — “the flexibility test”**

Plans change.

They always do.

You stay later than expected.
You feel rough.
Weather turns.

Car?

Doesn’t matter.

Bike + train?

Now you’re working around timetables, routes, and energy.

It’s not just travel anymore.

It’s logistics.

---

## **Where it *does* win (be fair, sharp)**

To be clear — in the right scenario, it’s unbeatable:

* commuter corridors
* reliable trains
* short, safe cycle links

In those cases?

It’s faster than driving.
Cheaper than driving.
Less stressful than both.

---

## **But most people aren’t in that scenario**

Most people have:

* slightly awkward routes
* slightly unreliable transport
* slightly too much distance

Individually, fine.

Together?

Enough to break the system.

---

## **The core problem (clean insight)**

This bike doesn’t fail because it’s bad.

It fails because it’s *optimised*.

And most people don’t live optimised lives.

---

## **Return to hook (payoff)**

Remember that awkward commute?

This can fix it.

Completely.

Turn 90 minutes into 50.
Turn dead time into usable time.
Save thousands doing it.

---

## **Final turn (don’t buy it)**

But only if your life fits the system.

Not the other way round.

Because if you have to force it…
if you’re constantly adjusting…

you won’t use it.

And something you don’t use—

No matter how clever—

Is just expensive clutter.

---

## **Ending line (tight, memorable)**

It’s not about the bike.

It’s about whether your life is predictable enough…

…for something this smart to actually work.
