
**"DON'T BUY A BROMPTON"**

---

Mark commutes into Manchester every day.

Door to door: 55 minutes. Train, a ten minute cycle at the other end. Brompton under his desk by 9am.

He saves roughly £3,000 a year compared to running a car. [verify]

He gets back around 200 hours annually he'd otherwise spend sitting in traffic. [verify — based on average commute stats]

He hasn't owned a car in six years.

My friend Sarah bought the same bike three months ago.

It's in her hallway.

She's used it once.

Same bike. £950. Completely different outcome.

And if you're thinking about buying one — you need to know which one of those people you actually are. Before you spend the money.

---

First. Why it's genuinely brilliant.

The fold is the thing that separates it from every other folding bike. Most compact on the market — [Andrew Ritchie designed it in his London flat in the late 1970s and it's barely changed because it was right the first time — verify]. Fits in a car boot, under a desk, in the overhead luggage rack. Goes on trains without a reservation. Goes on buses. Gets carried into the office.

That last bit matters more than it sounds — because it means no locking it up outside. Which means no coming back to find it gone.

But more than the practicalities — it changes the maths of your day.

Driving, you're occupied. Stressed, usually. Stuck behind someone who apparently passed their test in a different century.

On a train with a Brompton folded at your feet — you're reading. Thinking. Actually present. Those hours are yours again.

Over a year, that's weeks of your life.

---

Now the honest bit.

Because almost every Brompton review glosses over this, and it's the reason my friend Sarah's is in her hallway.

**Cycling in England is scary.**

Not in a dramatic way. In a quiet, persistent, this-road-has-no-cycle-lane-and-that-van-is-very-close way.

Small wheels don't help. A Brompton is brilliant for urban hops and station connections. On a fast A-road in the rain at 7pm it is a different experience entirely.

And if you're anything like me — I've commuted by bike, cracked a helmet, watched someone get hit by a car — you don't need convincing that the risk is real. Most routes in most English towns are not designed for this. They're designed for cars. Because everything in England is designed for cars.

---

Then there's the thing nobody puts in a review.

It's a bit embarrassing to ride.

The small wheels. The upright position. The way it looks next to a normal bike. People notice. Occasionally they say something — usually teenagers, always unhelpful.

That sounds like something you should just get over.

And maybe you can. Some people genuinely don't register it.

But if you're the kind of person who does — and you know if you are — it matters. Because it's the difference between using it and not using it. And a bike you don't use is just an expensive piece of hallway furniture.

---

The flexibility problem is real too and nobody talks about it.

Once you've committed to bike-plus-train, you're committed. No nipping back if you forgot something. No flexibility if you feel rough halfway through the day. If you have any kind of health condition that flares unpredictably — IBS, migraines, anything — a journey that relies on cycling at both ends is suddenly a source of anxiety rather than freedom.

And then there's everywhere you'd want to take it that won't let you bring it in.

Post office — no. Most shops — no. The library — no. You end up standing outside Boots trying to lock a Brompton to a lamppost, getting looks, which brings us back to the embarrassment thing.

---

Here's the actual test.

Write down your five most common journeys. Not hypothetical ones — real ones. The badminton on Tuesday. The family visits. The supermarket run.

Now ask, honestly: does a Brompton help with any of these, specifically? Not in theory. On the actual roads, at the actual times, in the actual weather.

For each one where the answer is yes — one point.

Two or more: buy it. Your life has the right shape for this bike.

One or zero: it'll be in your hallway within a month. Sell it to someone whose life does.

---

The situations where it's genuinely unbeatable:

Train somewhere, cycle the last mile at the other end. Get picked up by someone without a bike rack — fold it, boot, done. Cycle to a place where you can bring it inside. Bus to somewhere, cycle the rest.

The situations where a regular bike does the job just as well and costs a third of the price:

Cycling to the local sports centre. Popping to the supermarket. Anything within a couple of miles of home on quiet roads.

The Brompton's magic is specifically in the combination — bike plus public transport, seamlessly. If your journeys don't have that combination in them, you're paying for a feature you'll never use.

---

Mark knew exactly what his journeys looked like before he bought it.

He'd mapped the routes. Checked the train times. Done the maths.

My friend Sarah bought one hoping her habits would change to fit the bike.

They didn't. The bike changed to fit her hallway.
