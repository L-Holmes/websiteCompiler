
## **Title:**

**“This 2,000-year-old idea could fix your commute. Don’t use it.”**

---

## **Intro (HOOK — historical story first)**

Two thousand years ago, the Romans built the fastest delivery system on Earth.

No, they didn't have folding bikes, or even bikes for that matter.

It was a variation of a transport they had.

But it wasn't with faster horses.

But by *swapping* them.

Ride hard. Stop. New horse. Keep going.

Over and over.

The system was called the Cursus Publicus.

And it was absurdly efficient.

If everything worked.

---

## **Snap to present (clean jump)**

We’re still doing the exact same thing.

Just with slightly less impressive horses.

---

## **Set-up (modern version of the same idea)**

Bike to a station.
Train into a city.
Bike the last bit.

Switching modes.

On paper, it’s perfect.

Faster than driving.
Cheaper than driving.
And — crucially — it gives you your time back.

---

## **Time (hit early, sharp, memorable)**

Because driving doesn’t just cost money.

It costs *time you can’t use*.

You’re locked in.
Focused. Slightly stressed.

Train?

Different story.

You can work. Watch something. Switch off.

That middle chunk of your journey becomes actual life.

Do that every day and you’re not saving minutes.

You’re gaining **days** back every year.

---

## **Money (quick, no drag)**

Then there’s cost.

Car:

~£3,600 a year, easily.

Fuel alone is ridiculous.

Folding bike?

~£1,000 upfront.

Add trains — still often cheaper overall.

So the pitch is simple:

**Spend less. Gain time.**

---

## **Introduce the Brompton (but not overly reverent)**

And the best tool for this?

A Brompton.

It folds properly small.

Not “folding bike small” —
*fits anywhere, no arguments, no hassle* small.

Which means you can actually do the switching:

* bike → train
* train → bike
* car boot → bike

It removes friction from the transitions.

Which is the whole game.

---

## **Reinforce with history (tight callback)**

It’s the same idea as the Romans.

Don’t rely on one method.

Switch between them.

That’s how you go faster.

---

## **Turn (shift tone slightly darker)**

But there’s a reason most people weren’t using the Cursus Publicus.

---

## **Why the Roman system actually worked (key insight)**

It only worked because:

* the routes were maintained
* the stations were reliable
* everything was predictable

If one part failed…

The whole system slowed down.

---

## **Bring it to modern reality (sharp comparison)**

Now apply that to today.

* trains delayed
* roads not built for cycling
* weather doing whatever it wants

Your “perfect system” starts to wobble.

And when one part wobbles…

You feel it immediately.

---

## **Victorian Britain (quick supporting example)**

Even in Victorian Britain, people were doing this.

Carriage to the station.
Train.
Another carriage at the other end.

Same idea.

And they ran into the same issue:

Miss one link…

…and the whole journey falls apart.

---

## **Modern friction (fast, grounded examples)**

Because real life isn’t clean.

You’re tired.
It’s raining.
You’re running late.

Now instead of one decision — drive or not —

You’ve got a chain:

* cycle
* fold
* train
* unfold
* cycle again

Each step small.

Together?

Friction.

---

## **Flexibility (important contrast)**

A car is inefficient…

…but brutally flexible.

Leave whenever.
Change plans instantly.
Carry anything.

This system?

It’s efficient —
but conditional.

---

## **Where it *does* work (credibility)**

And to be fair:

If your route is:

* short cycle
* reliable train
* short cycle

This is unbeatable.

Genuinely.

Faster than driving.
Cheaper than driving.
Less stressful overall.

---

## **But here’s the catch (core truth)**

Most people aren’t in that situation.

They’re close.

But not quite.

And “not quite” is enough to break it.

---

## **The deeper insight (tight, logical)**

This kind of system is *optimised*.

It rewards consistency.

But most lives aren’t consistent.

Plans change.
Energy changes.
Conditions change.

And every time they do…

…the system gets harder to use.

---

## **Final callback to history (clean, satisfying)**

The Romans figured this out 2,000 years ago.

Switching transport is powerful.

It’s how you go faster.

It’s how you move efficiently.

But it only works…

…when the world around you is stable.

---

## **Ending (strong, memorable, slightly cynical)**

And that’s the problem.

Because the Brompton isn’t just a bike.

It’s a system.

And if your life doesn’t perfectly fit that system—

It doesn’t matter how clever it is.

It won’t save you time.
It won’t save you money.

It’ll just sit there…

waiting for a version of your life that doesn’t quite exist.
