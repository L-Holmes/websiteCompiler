

---

## **Title:**

**“This folding bike is genius. Don’t buy one.”**

---

## **Intro (HOOK — story first, no fluff)**

I almost got hit by a car once.

Not dramatic. Not cinematic. Just… a normal Tuesday.
Driver didn’t look. I didn’t trust the road enough. Close enough.

And that’s roughly the moment I realised:

This *brilliant*, clever, beautifully engineered folding bike…

…might be completely useless to me.

---

## **Set-up (promise + tension)**

Because on paper, the Brompton is perfect.

It can save you *thousands* a year.
It can literally give you *hours of your life back*.

It solves problems that normal bikes can’t even touch.

And yet…


---

## **What makes it genius (fast, punchy)**

This thing folds smaller than basically anything else.

Not “a bit smaller”.
Like — *fits under a desk, goes in a car boot, slides onto a train without a fight* small.

Which unlocks something most people miss:

You can mix transport.

Bike → train → bike
Bike → bus → bike
Bike → mate picks you up → done

A normal bike can’t do that.

---

## **Time argument (memorable + visual)**

Here’s the bit no one talks about properly:

Driving eats your time.

You can’t do anything. You’re locked in.

But on a train?

You can work. Watch something. Zone out.
It’s not travel time — it’s *usable life time*.

If you commute like that regularly, you’re not saving minutes…

You’re gaining *weeks* back over a year.

---

## **Money (quick, brutal numbers)**

Let’s be blunt.

A cheap car:

* £4,000 upfront → ~£500/year
* Insurance → ~£400
* Fuel → ~£2,300
* Maintenance → ~£400

That’s about **£3,600 a year**.

A second-hand Brompton?

~£950.

Even with trains and buses — you’re often still *way* under that.

So yeah.

Time up. Cost down.

Sounds like a no-brainer.

---

## **So why not just buy one? (turn)**

Because none of that matters…

If you don’t actually use it.

---

## **The real problem (story + emotion)**

I’ve owned mine for ages.

Do you know how many times I’ve used it alone?

Basically zero.

Not because it doesn’t work.

Because I don’t.

---

## **Embarrassment (main driver, honest + sharp)**

I’m just… embarrassed to ride it.

Not rational. Not logical.

But real.

You feel people looking.
You imagine someone laughing.
You can’t quite shake it.

And people will say:

“Mate, no one cares.”

They’re wrong.

Some people don’t care.
Some people absolutely do.

And crucially — *you don’t know which one you’re about to pass*.

That’s enough.

---

## **Cycling reality (UK-specific bluntness)**

Then there’s the roads.

Cycling sounds great until you’re:

* dodging parked cars
* squeezed by traffic
* soaked in the rain
* trying to not die on a roundabout

If I need to be somewhere at 7…

Am I doing a 1-hour drive?

Or a **2.5-hour** mess involving trains, cycling, and stress?

---

## **“Just use it locally” (kill the obvious counter)**

Alright — what about short trips?

Shops. Gym. Badminton.

Here’s the problem:

You *can’t bring it inside most places*.

Shops don’t want it.
Staff give you looks.
You feel like a nuisance.

So you lock it outside?

Cool — now you’ve got a £1,000 bike sitting there.

---

## **The awkward truth (fast bullets, no fluff)**

So in reality, it’s only useful for:

* train → bike
* bus → bike
* getting picked up by someone without a bike rack

That’s it.

Everything else?

A normal bike is easier.
Or a car is.

---

## **Personal contradiction (human, relatable)**

And this is the worst bit.

I’ll be sat there thinking:

“Ooh… this would be perfect for the Brompton.”

And then…

…I still won’t use it.

Because I know I won’t enjoy the ride there.

---

## **Pushback (defensive section, credibility)**

And before you say it—

I’ve cycled loads.

* Commuted daily
* Grew up riding everywhere
* Crashed, seen crashes, dealt with all of it

This isn’t fear of cycling.

It’s realism about where I live.

---

## **The bigger truth (zoom out)**

The problem isn’t the bike.

It’s everything around it.

* Roads built for cars
* Public transport that’s patchy
* Social pressure that sounds small but isn’t

If I lived somewhere quieter… flatter… more cycle-friendly…

I’d use it all the time.

---

## **Final decision (clean, decisive)**

But I don’t.

So I listed it.

Took the photos. Wrote the description.

Because the truth is:

A tool is only useful…

…if you actually use it.

---

## **Ending (loop back, memorable line)**

The Brompton is one of the smartest bits of design I’ve ever owned.

It saves time. Saves money. Solves real problems.

But none of that beats this:

If the idea of using it stops you leaving the house…

…it’s not a solution.

It’s clutter.
