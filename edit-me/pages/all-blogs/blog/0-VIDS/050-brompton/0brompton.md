## **Intro (HOOK — historical story first)**

Two thousand years ago, the Romans built the fastest delivery system on Earth.

No, they didn't have folding bikes, or even bikes for that matter.

It was a variation of a transport they had.

But it wasn't with faster horses.

But by *swapping* them.

Ride hard. Stop. New horse. Keep going.

Over and over.

The system was called the Cursus Publicus.

And it was absurdly efficient.

If everything worked.

### The Tethered Goat

The problem with a Brompton is that you can’t just *leave* it. 

Even with the best lock in the world, leaving a £1,000 folding bike outside a shop in 2026 is essentially a charitable donation to the local bolt-cutter enthusiast. So, you bring it inside. That’s the "ingenious" part, right?

Wrong. 

Imagine you’re in a crowded pub in Glasgow. You’ve had a brilliant evening, the atmosphere is electric, and everyone decides to move to the next spot. But you? You’re the guy with the 12kg anchor. You’re unfolding your life in the middle of a dark, sticky doorway while your friends disappear into the night. 

You aren't free. You’re a tethered goat. You’re constantly negotiating your space. You go into a post office or a chemist, and suddenly you’re "The Problem." You’re the person bumping into the shampoo display with a wet, heavy lump of steel. You’re faffing with latches while a queue of people stares at the back of your head. 

The design is perfect, but the world is built of narrow aisles and judgmental silence.


The Brompton’s greatest strength is its greatest weakness: you have to take it with you.

In 2026, leaving a £1,000 bike outside a shop is essentially a charitable donation to the local bolt-cutter enthusiast. So, you bring it inside. But the British high street wasn't designed for "ingenious folding solutions." 

Imagine you’re in a crowded pub. The night is going brilliantly, but then the group decides to move on. Suddenly, you aren't "a friend"; you’re "The Problem." You’re the guy in the sticky doorway, struggling with a latch, while your mates disappear into the night. 

You are a **Tethered Goat**. You can't just *be* somewhere; you have to negotiate your bike’s existence in every Post Office, Chemist, and Café. You are constantly apologising for 12kg of wet steel bumping into a shampoo display. 

---
i.e. The "Inside" Myth

The big sell is that you can take it inside. No locks, no theft.

But try taking a folded Brompton into a quiet post office. Or a chemist. You aren't "a person with a bike"—you are a "problem with wheels." You are constantly negotiating your space. You’re the guy faffing with a latch while three people queue behind you. 

I once took it on a night out. The "logical" plan: train, cycle to the pub, cycle to the hotel. 

I spent the entire night being "The Bike Guy." I couldn't stay for the last round because I had to navigate a city I didn't know on tiny wheels while everyone else hopped in an Uber. I wasn't free. I was tethered to a piece of engineering that I felt I had to defend.
