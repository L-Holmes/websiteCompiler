
**"THE TALLEST BUILDING YOU'VE NEVER THOUGHT ABOUT"**

---

In 1884, the people of Ulm did something that took 632 years.

They finished their church.

The spire — finally complete after six centuries of construction, delays, money running out, wars, general medieval chaos — reached 161 metres into the sky.

Tallest church in the world. Still is. [verify current ranking]

And almost nobody outside Germany has ever heard of it.

---

Here's the thing about spires.

They're a cheat code.

A narrow stone needle contributes almost nothing to the experience of a building. You can't be inside it. You can barely see it from street level without craning your neck at an angle that makes you feel slightly stupid.

But it absolutely destroys you in the height rankings.

Ulm without its spire is a perfectly respectable 62 metres. [verify]

That's a decent office block.

That's not "took 632 years."

---

The same trick — and I say trick warmly — is everywhere once you start looking.

One World Trade Centre in New York is officially 541 metres tall. Which makes it taller than the Willis Tower in Chicago, a building that for years was the undisputed tallest in America.

Except 124 of those metres are antenna. [verify]

A pole. An expensive, structurally significant, very tall pole with no floors in it.

There is an actual committee — a real organisation with a logo and an annual conference — that decides whether poles count.

They decided it counts.

Chicago has had feelings about this ever since.

---

I started thinking about all of this not because of skyscrapers or cathedrals, but because of a mast on a hill in Lancashire.

Winter Hill. There's a broadcasting mast up there that stands 309 metres tall — roughly the same height as the Shard. [verify]

I've driven past it dozens of times on the M61.

I have never once felt anything about it.

The Shard has a viewing platform with a 45-minute queue and a gift shop. The Winter Hill mast has a warning light so aircraft don't fly into it.

Same number. Completely different objects.

---

And that's when the geometry of it clicked for me.

Height is a measurement. Impressiveness is an experience. And the gap between them is bigger than you'd think.

When you're standing at the base of something, the amount of sky it displaces — the angle at which it fills your vision — depends just as much on width and mass as it does on how high the top point is.

A needle disappears. Your brain discounts it almost automatically.

But something wide, something that surrounds you, something that takes up *space* —

that lands differently.

Which is why Sagrada Família, at 172 metres, feels like it's arguing with the sky.

And the Ulm spire, 11 metres taller, just... points at it.

---

So I started wondering. If you took the spires away — stripped out the needles and the antennae and the poles — what's actually the most impressive structure in Britain?

And that's how I ended up reading about Liverpool Cathedral at half eleven on a Tuesday night and genuinely having a small moment.

---

Liverpool Cathedral is the largest cathedral in the UK.

Fine, you might think. Cathedrals are big, this one's the biggest, moving on.

But it's also one of the longest in the world — behind only St Peter's Basilica in Rome and the Basilica of Our Lady of Peace in Yamoussoukro, Ivory Coast. Both of which, with respect, feel like they're cheating slightly. [verify — St Peter's and Yamoussoukro comparisons]

The height of the central tower — no spire, no antenna, no needle — is 101 metres of solid sandstone. [verify]

That's the actual building. That's all structure you can be inside, walk around, look up through.

And it was designed by a 21-year-old.

---

Giles Gilbert Scott submitted his winning design in 1903.

He was 21.

He had never built anything before.

The judges liked his design so much they gave him the commission and then quietly assigned him a more experienced architect to work alongside, presumably because they'd looked at his CV and had a mild panic.

[verify — Scott's age at commission is well documented, the detail about the supervising architect is worth confirming]

Scott didn't live to see it finished. The cathedral took 74 years to complete — broken up by two world wars, endless funding struggles, a city that was being bombed while they were still laying stone.

It was consecrated in 1978.

Scott died in 1960.

---

I read all of this and closed my laptop and opened it again immediately.

A *local* cathedral. Forty minutes down the M6. One of the largest buildings in the world by internal volume. Designed by a 21-year-old who'd never built anything.

I had to go.

So I got in the car.

Went back inside to get my camera.

Camera wasn't charged.

Found a spare battery.

Forty-five minutes behind schedule —

*[show entirely wrong route on the map]*

---

And here it is.

I mean.

Just — look at the size of those buttresses.

---

There's a version of this video that's just about measurements and rankings and which building technically beats which other building on a spreadsheet.

But that's not really what it's about.

It's about the fact that the most extraordinary building in England is sitting on a hill in Liverpool, and most people drive past the signs for it their whole lives without ever going in.

Not because it isn't remarkable.

Just because nobody told them to look up.
