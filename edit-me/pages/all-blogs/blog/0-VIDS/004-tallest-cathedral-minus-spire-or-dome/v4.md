We've been competing on height for a very long time.

The medieval cathedral builders had a system. You want your city to matter — really matter, the kind of mattering that people could see from ten miles away on a clear day — you build a spire.

Not a tower. Not a wall. A spire.

Because a spire is essentially a cheat code for the height rankings. Narrow enough to contribute almost nothing to the experience of the building, tall enough to win the argument.

And it almost didn't exist at all.

When the competition for the commission opened in 1903, nobody expected the winning entry to come from a 22-year-old with nothing on his CV.

Giles Gilbert Scott had never built anything.

What followed was — and there's no more precise word for it — a life's work.

Scott spent 57 years on this building. Through two world wars. Through the Blitz, which bombed the city his cathedral was slowly rising above.
