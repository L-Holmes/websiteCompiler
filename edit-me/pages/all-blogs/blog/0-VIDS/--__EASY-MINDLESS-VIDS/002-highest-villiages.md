
The highest permanent town on Earth is so high that just walking up stairs can make your lungs bleed.

Not metaphorically.

Actually.

At 5,100 meters above sea level, La Rinconada in Peru sits almost as high as Everest Base Camp. Except unlike a climbing camp, this place has schools. Shops. Internet cafés. Children playing football in freezing thin air.

And around 50,000 people live there year-round.

Which raises an obvious question:

Why?

Because humans are not designed for this.

At these altitudes, there’s roughly half the oxygen you’d get at sea level. Your body starts malfunctioning almost immediately. Headaches. nausea. insomnia. confusion. Your heart works harder just to keep you alive.

Tourists struggle to walk.

People here carry groceries uphill.

And the first thing you notice about La Rinconada is that it barely looks real.

The town clings to the edge of a glacier beneath permanently grey skies. Buildings stack unevenly across muddy frozen slopes. The roads aren’t really roads — more like churned-up tracks of ice and dirt filled with mining runoff.

It looks temporary.

Like a settlement that shouldn’t survive a single winter.

But it does.

Because underneath the mountain is gold.

That’s the reason the city exploded in size. Tens of thousands of miners moved upward chasing rumors of wealth buried inside the glaciers surrounding the town. And over time, an economy emerged in one of the harshest environments humans permanently inhabit.

There’s almost no sanitation infrastructure.

The cold is brutal.

Oxygen levels are brutal.

The work is brutal.

Yet every morning, people still wake up there and go to work like it’s normal.

Which is maybe the strangest part of high-altitude settlements. Humans normalize absolutely anything.

But La Rinconada isn’t alone.

In Tibet, there’s Wenquan.

And this place feels even less believable.

Because unlike dramatic mountain cities tourists photograph constantly, Wenquan is isolated in this enormous empty high-altitude landscape where the horizon itself feels elevated.

At nearly 4,900 meters above sea level, the environment becomes psychologically strange. The light is harsher. Shadows feel sharper. Distances become deceptive because there are so few trees or landmarks to judge scale.

Even breathing while standing still feels slightly wrong.

Yet people herd livestock here year-round.

Entire communities adapted to conditions that would leave most visitors exhausted within minutes.

And high altitude changes humans in fascinating ways.

People born in these regions often develop larger lung capacity. Different blood oxygen processing. Bodies literally adapted across generations to survive environments where oxygen barely exists.

You can see it in daily life.

Children running around effortlessly while outsiders stop every few steps trying not to black out.

That’s what makes these villages feel so surreal. Not just their height — their normality.

Because after a while, even the impossible becomes routine.

In Korzok, India, that normality looks almost peaceful.

The village sits beside a high Himalayan lake surrounded by barren mountains that look almost Martian in certain lighting. Winters are viciously cold. Temperatures collapse far below freezing. Roads become unreliable. Supplies become difficult.

And people still stay.

Not seasonally.

Permanently.

There’s a tendency to imagine extreme places as dramatic survival stories every second of every day. But most of the time, life in these villages is surprisingly ordinary.

Kids go to school.

People argue.

Someone’s probably annoyed about their neighbor.

That contrast is what makes these places fascinating.

The environment feels completely hostile to human existence… yet humans immediately start doing human things there anyway.

Making tea.

Building homes.

Raising families.

Complaining about Wi-Fi.

And once you notice that pattern, you see it everywhere.

Because humans are weirdly unwilling to leave mountains alone.

There are permanent settlements in the Andes, the Himalayas, the Tibetan Plateau — places where agriculture barely functions, winters become lethal, and oxygen deprivation is constant.

And historically, many existed for practical reasons.

Trade routes.

Isolation.

Religion.

Defense.

Mining.

But eventually communities become self-sustaining simply because generations are born there. A place stops feeling extreme once it’s home.

That’s the real difference between visitors and residents.

Visitors experience altitude as suffering.

Residents experience it as weather.

Which becomes incredibly obvious watching footage from La Rinconada. Tourists arrive visibly struggling for breath while locals casually carry equipment uphill through muddy snow.

Your body screams this place is wrong.

Theirs has adapted.

And adaptation at altitude comes with tradeoffs.

Higher rates of chronic mountain sickness. Increased strain on the heart. Lower oxygen saturation permanently affecting the body over time.

Humans can survive here.

That doesn’t mean it’s easy.

Which almost makes these settlements feel more impressive than giant monuments or ancient cities.

Because unlike abandoned ruins, people are still actively choosing to live here right now.

Tonight, while most humans sleep close to sea level with comfortable oxygen-rich air, entire communities will sleep nearly five kilometers above it.

Children will wake up tomorrow in towns literally higher than most mountains in Europe.

Dogs will bark.

People will open shops.

Someone will complain about work.

All in environments that would make most outsiders physically collapse.

And maybe that’s the strangest thing about humanity.

Give us an uninhabitable mountain…

…and eventually somebody will call it home.
