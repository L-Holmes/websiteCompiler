
There’s a place where the ocean disappears.

Twice a day.

Miles of water just… gone.

This is the Bay of Fundy—and it has the highest tides on Earth.

The difference between high and low tide here can reach 16 meters.
That’s a four-story building… in water.

So at high tide, you’ve got boats floating peacefully.

And a few hours later—

They’re sitting on mud. Completely stranded.

Same place. Same boat. No water.

It looks like the sea just drained away.

But it didn’t.

It moved.

The shape of the bay acts like a funnel—forcing incoming ocean water to pile up higher and higher as it rushes in.

Then gravity pulls it all back out again.

Over. And over.

Which means the coastline isn’t fixed.

It’s breathing.

In. Out.

You can literally walk on the ocean floor—see fish flopping, seaweed drying, the ground where waves were just hours ago.

But here’s the strange part—

Nothing there treats it as unusual.

Crabs dig into the mud like it’s normal.
Birds time their feeding to the tides.
Boats are built to fall over.

Because here, “land” and “sea”… aren’t separate things.

They’re just the same place—

taking turns.
