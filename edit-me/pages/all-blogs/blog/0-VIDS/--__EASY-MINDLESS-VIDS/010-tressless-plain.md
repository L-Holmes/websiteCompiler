

**Variation 1 — The 1,200km Treeless Plain**

No rivers.
No hills.
No trees.

Just rock.

For over 200,000 square kilometers.

This is the Nullarbor Plain—and at first glance, it looks like something is missing.

Because landscapes aren’t supposed to be this empty.

You expect variation.

Elevation.

Water carving paths through terrain.

But here—

nothing breaks the surface.

It’s flat.

Relentlessly flat.

And that’s not because nothing ever happened here.

It’s because too much did.

Millions of years ago, this entire region was underwater.

A shallow inland sea.

Warm, full of marine life—

shells, coral, microscopic organisms building layer after layer on the seafloor.

And when those organisms died—

they didn’t disappear.

They accumulated.

Compressed.

Turned into limestone.

Kilometers of it.

Which means this entire landscape—

what looks like empty ground—

is actually made from ancient life.

Packed together over time.

But that alone doesn’t explain the flatness.

Because limestone landscapes usually aren’t flat.

They’re full of caves, sinkholes, sharp formations—

irregular, broken, complex.

Karst terrain.

And this *is* karst.

One of the largest karst systems on Earth.

But here’s the difference—

you can’t see most of it.

Because instead of forming dramatic surface features—

everything happened underground.

Rainwater—slightly acidic—

slowly dissolved the limestone over millions of years.

Carving out caves.

Tunnels.

Voids stretching for vast distances beneath the surface.

Water didn’t stay above ground long enough to form rivers.

It drained.

Straight down.

Which is why there are no rivers here.

No lakes.

No surface water systems at all.

Because the moment water arrives—

it disappears.

Pulled underground into a network you can’t see.

Which creates a strange effect.

The land above stays dry.

Extremely dry.

Because it can’t hold onto water.

And without water—

you don’t get trees.

You don’t get dense vegetation.

You don’t get much at all.

Just low, sparse plant life adapted to survive with almost nothing.

Which reinforces the emptiness.

No trees means no vertical structure.

No shadows breaking the horizon.

Nothing to interrupt the view.

So the flatness becomes overwhelming.

Total.

But it’s not actually featureless.

It just hides its features differently.

Because beneath your feet—

there are cave systems stretching for kilometers.

Some of the longest underwater caves in the world.

Completely invisible from above.

Which means this place isn’t empty—

it’s inverted.

All the complexity is below you.

And all the simplicity is on the surface.

But there’s another layer to this.

Because over time, something else happened.

The sea retreated.

The land uplifted slightly.

And then—

erosion stopped doing what it normally does.

Because there were no rivers to carve valleys.

No flowing water shaping the land.

Just slow chemical weathering.

Which smooths things out.

Reduces variation instead of increasing it.

So instead of becoming more rugged over time—

this landscape became more uniform.

Flattened.

Simplified.

Which is unusual.

Because most landscapes become more complex as they age.

More eroded. More varied.

This one did the opposite.

It lost detail.

Over millions of years.

And what’s left

is one of the most consistent surfaces on Earth.

Which is why roads here can stretch perfectly straight for hundreds of kilometers.

There’s nothing to go around.

Nothing to adjust for.

Just space.

And that space does something to perception.

Because when there are no reference points—

distance becomes hard to judge.

The horizon feels fixed.

Objects don’t seem to move closer.

Time feels stretched.

Which makes crossing this place feel different from crossing anywhere else.

Not because of danger—

but because of absence.

There’s nothing to engage with.

Nothing to break the continuity.

Just repetition.

Sky.

Ground.

Again and again.

And that repetition reveals something.

Because it shows what a landscape looks like

when almost every shaping force has been removed.

No rivers.

No mountains.

No forests.

Just geology.

Raw and exposed.

And once you see it like that—

the emptiness stops feeling like something is missing.

Because this isn’t a landscape that failed to develop.

It’s one that was stripped back.

Refined.

Until only the most stable elements remained.

Limestone.

Flatness.

Dryness.

Everything else—

gone.

Which means the real question isn’t

“why is there nothing here?”

It’s

“what had to happen

for so much

to disappear?”
