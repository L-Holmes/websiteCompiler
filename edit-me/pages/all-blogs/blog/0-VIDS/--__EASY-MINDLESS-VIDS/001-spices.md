Why Your Spice Rack Was Worth More Than Gold  


If you open your kitchen cupboard right now, you probably have a jar of nutmeg. It costs about two dollars. But in the 1600s, this little wrinkled seed was the single most contested resource on the planet. It was worth more than its weight in gold.

Nutmeg only grew in one place on Earth: The Banda Islands. A tiny, incredibly remote volcanic archipelago in modern-day Indonesia.

If you were a European merchant, getting it meant sailing for months, surviving scurvy, pirates, and shipwrecks. But if you made it back with a single ship full of nutmeg, you were set for life. The Dutch and the British fought bloody, brutal wars over these tiny islands.

In fact, to secure a monopoly on nutmeg, the Dutch formally traded a different island they owned to the British. That island was Manhattan. New York City was traded for nutmeg.

The entire map of the modern world was quite literally drawn by people desperately looking for a cheaper way to flavor their dinner. Because before global shipping, geography dictated your diet.

Take black pepper. Today, it’s on every restaurant table in the world. But originally, the black pepper vine was entirely native to the Malabar Coast of India, specifically Kerala.

To get to Europe, it had to pass through dozens of middlemen—Arab traders, Venetian merchants—each jacking up the price. It was so valuable that when Alaric the Goth laid siege to Rome in the year 410, his ransom demand to spare the city wasn't just gold and silver. He demanded—and received—three thousand pounds of black pepper.

And because Europe was so entirely disconnected from where these spices actually grew, the merchants who controlled the trade simply made things up to keep the prices high.

Cinnamon comes from the inner bark of a specific tree native to Sri Lanka. But for centuries, Arab traders told the Greeks and Romans that cinnamon was gathered by giant, terrifying birds who used the spice to build their nests on sheer, unclimbable cliffs. The traders claimed they had to leave heavy chunks of meat out for the birds, who would carry the meat to their nests, collapsing them, and sending the cinnamon tumbling down.

It was complete nonsense. But the Europeans believed it, and happily paid the monster-bird premium.

By the late 1400s, European powers were sick of paying these markups. So, they started sailing west, looking for a backdoor to India.

Which brings us to Christopher Columbus. Columbus lands in the Americas, thinks he's in India, and is desperately looking for black pepper to justify the cost of his voyage. He doesn't find it. But he does find a completely unrelated, violently spicy red fruit native to Mesoamerica.

He calls them "peppers" to make his investors happy. And unlike the fussy black pepper vines of India, these new "chili peppers" grew almost anywhere. Within fifty years, they had spread across the globe, completely revolutionizing the cuisines of India, China, and Southeast Asia.

So the next time you season your food, look at what you’re holding. You aren't just holding dried bark and seeds. You are holding the remnants of global monopolies, the fall of empires, and the reason the map of the world looks the way it does today.
