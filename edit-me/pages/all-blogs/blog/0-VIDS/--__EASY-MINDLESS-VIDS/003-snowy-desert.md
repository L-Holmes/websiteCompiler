
It snows in the Gobi Desert.
Not rarely. Regularly.

Which sounds wrong, because deserts are supposed to be hot.

But deserts aren’t defined by heat.
They’re defined by lack of precipitation.

The Gobi gets barely any moisture all year—so when it *does* arrive, it doesn’t fall as rain.

It falls as snow.

Because the Gobi is cold. Brutally cold.

In winter, temperatures drop below −30°C.
That’s colder than parts of Antarctica during summer.

And there’s almost nothing to trap heat—no forests, no humidity, no cloud cover.

So the little warmth that arrives during the day?
It just escapes at night. Straight back into space.

Which is why you get this—

A frozen desert.

You’ll see herds of Bactrian camel walking through snowstorms.
Animals built for heat… surviving extreme cold instead.

Because they’re not built for heat.
They’re built for *scarcity*.

Their humps don’t store water—they store fat.
Energy. Backup fuel for when nothing grows for months.

And in the Gobi, that matters more than temperature ever will.

But here’s the strange part—

The snow doesn’t stay.

Not because it melts—because it disappears.

Dry air pulls moisture straight out of the snow in a process called sublimation—turning ice directly into vapor.

No puddles. No slush. Just… gone.

So you can have a desert.
Covered in snow.
With animals walking through it—

And still have almost no usable water.

Which means the snow isn’t relief.

It’s temporary.

A brief moment where the desert looks like something else—

before the air quietly takes it all back.
