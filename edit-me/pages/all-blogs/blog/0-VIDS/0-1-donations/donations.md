Here's the ranking based on effect sizes reported in the paper (measured as *r*, where higher = stronger effect):

## 1. 🥇 Pique Technique — *r = 0.49 (total revenue)*

**What it is:** Asking for an unusual, specific amount of money (e.g., "17 cents" instead of "20 cents") to break a donor's automatic refusal habit and spark curiosity about *why* that amount is needed.

**Quote:** the technique involved asking donors for "unusual amounts of money (17c instead of 10c)... designed to break the 'refusal script.'"

**Charity example:** Instead of "Please donate £10," a charity mailer says "We need exactly £13.47 to provide one week of meals for a child."

**YouTube example:** Instead of "please support me on Patreon," a creator says: "For just $7 a month — exactly what it costs me to host one video — you can keep this channel going." Viewers pause, get curious, and engage.
YouTube Example: A creator ending a video by asking viewers to consider supporting their Patreon for exactly $2.34 a month, explaining that the specific number covers the exact bandwidth costs of hosting their community server.

---

## 2. 🥈 Prosocial Modelling — *r = 0.22*

**What it is:** Showing people *seeing others donate* makes them significantly more likely to donate themselves. Crucially, the model must perform the exact behaviour (donating), not just generally "be nice."

**Quote:** "When people saw others acting prosocially, they were more likely to imitate, including charitable donations... Effects were consistent across media, age, gender, and culture."

**Charity example:** A fundraising video shows real donors saying "I just gave £20" rather than just celebrities saying "poverty is bad."

**YouTube example:** A creator reads out Patreon supporters on screen: "Thanks to Jake, Sarah, and 47 others who joined this month." Viewers see real peers donating, not just an abstract ask.
Using a live stream overlay where every time someone donates via Super Chat, their name, custom message, and the exact amount they gave pop up on screen for the thousands of other viewers to see.

## 4. Being Observed / Donation Visibility — *r = 0.15*

**What it is:** People donate more when they are seen doing it. Making donations public — showing names, amounts, or donor lists — significantly boosts giving. Artificial "watching eyes" images do *not* work reliably.

**Quote:** "being observed significantly increased donations... effects were larger for repeat interactions... and where observation is more intense."

**Charity example:** A public donor wall or leaderboard ("Today's top donors: James £500, Maria £200...") displayed at a fundraising event increases giving meaningfully.

**YouTube example:** A creator publicly thanks members in every video and shows a live supporter ticker on stream. Donors are seen — and others want to be seen too.

## 5. Identifiable / Individual Victim — *r = 0.13*

**What it is:** Presenting a single, named, specific person in need raises more money than showing statistics or groups. The more individual and concrete the beneficiary, the higher the donation.

**Quote:** "donation size increased when donors were presented with a single, 'identifiable victim'... than when presented with statistics or multiple recipients."

**Charity example:** "Meet Amara, age 6, who walks 3 miles to school with no shoes" outperforms "10 million children lack basic school supplies."

**YouTube example:** A creator making videos about a cause features *one specific person* their fundraiser is helping: "This month, we're raising money specifically for Daniel, a 19-year-old who..." rather than "helping thousands of people."

## 3. 🥉 Legitimising Paltry Contributions (compliance boost only) — *r = 0.22 compliance, but net revenue effect ≈ r = 0.03*

**What it is:** Phrases like "even a penny will help" significantly increase the *number* of people who donate — but this is almost entirely cancelled out because individual donation amounts drop. Good for growing donor numbers, bad for total revenue.

**Quote:** "a moderate increase in compliance... was offset by a decrease in the size of the average donation... The net effect... was a non-significant increase in total revenue."

**Charity example:** A street fundraiser saying "even a small amount helps" gets more people to stop and donate something, but the average gift shrinks.

**YouTube example:** "Even $1 a month helps keep the channel ad-free" gets more Patreon sign-ups, but the average pledge is much lower — worth it mainly to grow the supporter count.

---

## 7. Door-in-the-Face — *r = 0.08 (non-significant)*

**What it is:** First asking for a very large amount ("Could you donate £500?"), then retreating to a smaller ask ("Even £10 would help") to make the smaller amount feel reasonable by comparison. Evidence is *weak* — people say they'll give more but don't actually follow through.

**Quote:** "donors may be marginally more likely to say they will donate... but this does not translate into actual compliance."

**Charity example:** A phone fundraiser opens with "Would you consider a £200 monthly gift?" and after refusal asks "Could you manage £10?" Slightly more yeses, but not reliably more actual donations.

**YouTube example:** "Would you consider a $50/month Patreon tier?... No worries — even the $5 tier means the world." Slightly more conversions at the lower tier, but the evidence says don't rely on it.

---

## 8. Matching / Contribution Matching — *r = 0.08 (non-significant)*

**What it is:** Telling donors their contribution will be matched (e.g., "every pound you give, we'll match") had a small and *statistically insignificant* effect in controlled experiments.

**Quote:** "when donors were told their funds would be matched... there was a small but non-significant increase in donations."

**Charity example:** "A generous donor will match all gifts this week pound for pound!" — very commonly used, but the evidence suggests it's less powerful than assumed.

**YouTube example:** "A sponsor has agreed to match all Patreon sign-ups this week!" — feels compelling, but may not move the needle as much as creators hope.

### **Fun (and Slightly Brutal) Facts About Creator Donations**

* **The Harsh Reality:** Building an audience is tough. Out of 139,366 campaigns analyzed, a massive **115,183 (about 82%) received absolutely $0**.
* **The 1% Club:** Only about **250 creators (the top 1%)** in this dataset were making more than $2,500 a month—the threshold the study considers enough to actually make a living.
* **The Averages:** For the campaigns that *did* get support, the median monthly peak was just **$31**. Only 5% of active campaigns made more than $750 a month.
* **The "Same-Day Regret":** Overall, 29% of all pledges eventually get canceled. But crazily, **1 out of 4 cancellations happen on the exact same day** the pledge was made! Half of all cancellations happen within the first month.
* **The Generous Whales:** While the average patron pledges about $11.18 per campaign (totaling $23 a month across two creators), the highest single monthly pledge recorded in this data was a whopping **$25,000**.
* **Beyond the Goal:** The crowd doesn't stop just because you hit your target. For a quarter of the top 5% of creators, their monthly pledges actually exceed the maximum funding goal they set for themselves.

### **The Most Important Factors for Campaign Success (Ranked High to Low)**

Based on the study's regression analysis (Table 2), success on a platform like Patreon is heavily tied to "communication quality." Here is the hierarchy of what actually drives pledges and higher donation amounts:

**1. A Strong "Summary" Text**
* **The Data:** Having a summary text had the absolute highest correlation with getting any pledges at all (a coefficient of `0.63`). It also had a highly significant positive impact on the total amount pledged and the likelihood of reaching a goal. 
* **The Takeaway:** Your "elevator pitch" matters most. Viewers need to know exactly what you do and why they should support you within the first few seconds of reading.

**2. Campaign Images**
* **The Data:** Uploading an image was the second most powerful driver for getting your first pledge (coefficient of `0.57`) and was one of the strongest factors for increasing the actual *amount* of money pledged (coefficient of `0.33`).
* **The Takeaway:** Visuals are non-negotiable. Campaigns that look visually professional and appealing are far more likely to convert viewers into paying patrons.

**3. Linking Your Social Media**
* **The Data:** Connecting your social media had a massive impact on getting those initial pledges (coefficient of `0.49`) and reaching campaign goals. 
* **The Takeaway:** People want to know you are a real creator with an active community. Linking socials proves you have an ecosystem outside of just asking for money. 

**4. "Thank You" Content (Texts and Videos)**
* **The Data:** Having a dedicated "Thanks text" (coefficient `0.45`) or a "Thanks video" (coefficient `0.28`) strongly predicted whether a campaign got pledges. Interestingly, a Thank You video was highly effective at driving up the *total amount* pledged.
* **The Takeaway:** Gratitude pays off. Setting up automated, personalized thank-you messages or unlisted videos for new patrons makes them feel valued and validates their decision to open their wallets. 

**5. The "About" Text (Long-form description)**
* **The Data:** Having an expanded "About" section was very effective for securing initial pledges (coefficient `0.32`) and significantly increased total pledge amounts.
* **The Takeaway:** While the quick summary hooks them, the deeper "About" section convinces the super-fans to actually commit. 

**6. Setting Goals (With a Catch)**
* **The Data:** Just having a goal helps attract pledges (coefficient `0.18`). However, the data showed that having *too many* goals actually hurts your overall pledge amount (negative correlation of `-0.021`). Furthermore, setting your goal amount too high negatively impacts your chances of reaching it.
* **The Takeaway:** Set a goal so people know what they are funding, but keep it realistic and keep the number of milestones low. Cluttering your page with 10 different financial goals overwhelms people.

**7. Being a Female Creator**
* **The Data:** The study noted a slight statistical advantage for female creators in attracting *any* initial pledges (coefficient `0.18`) compared to male creators. 
* **The Takeaway:** While there was an edge in getting off the ground, the researchers noted there was "no gender difference in the pledged amount" overall. 

**8. Having a Pitch Video**
* **The Data:** Surprisingly, having a main campaign video had a very weak correlation with getting pledges (`0.047`) and practically zero impact on the total amount pledged. 
* **The Takeaway:** While a "Thank You" video works wonders, stressing out over a highly produced pitch video might not be worth the effort compared to just writing a really good summary and having great graphics.

### **Psychological Drivers of Donations (Ranked High to Low)**

Based on the regression analysis (Table 5), here is what actually moves the needle for donation intent. We use the Beta ($\beta$) value to show the strength of the influence.

#### **1. Enjoyment / Entertainment**
* **Effectiveness:** Highest ($\beta = 0.829$)
* **The Quote:** > *"When presented with engaging and entertaining content, audiences are more likely to donate. Therefore, creators should focus on producing high-quality content that can captivate audiences. This can include the use of humor, storytelling..."*
* **Quick Example:** Creating content that is genuinely fun, funny, or high-quality.
* **Charity Example:** A local zoo hosting a "Comedy Night for the Critters" where the primary draw is the entertainment, and the donation is a byproduct of the fun.
* **YouTube Example:** A gaming creator who spends hours editing a 10-minute video to ensure every second has a joke or a high-stakes moment, rather than just uploading raw gameplay.

#### **2. Social Interaction**
* **Effectiveness:** Moderate ($\beta = 0.358$)
* **The Quote:** > *"Creators should actively engage their audiences through real-time interactions, such as responding to comments, hosting Q&A sessions, and encouraging audience participation..."*
* **Quick Example:** Making the viewer feel "seen" through two-way communication.
* **Charity Example:** A non-profit hosting a "Call-In Day" where donors can talk directly to the field researchers they are funding.

#### **3. Social Identity**
* **Effectiveness:** Lower-Moderate ($\beta = 0.178$)

* **Charity Example:** A charity for veterans that builds a private "Vets-Only" community hub for donors to share stories, reinforcing their shared identity.
* **YouTube Example:** A creator giving their fanbase a specific name (e.g., "The Nerdfighters" or "The Bread Gang") and creating inside jokes that only "true fans" understand.

* **Effectiveness:** Lowest significant factor ($\beta = 0.141$)

### 1. 🥇 Participatory Interaction → Emotional Loyalty
*γ = 0.63 — the strongest path in the entire model*

Users who like, share, repost, collect, and engage with content develop emotional loyalty to that creator. This is one-sided behaviour from the viewer — they don't even need a response — but it builds the deepest long-term commitment.

**YouTube example:** Every time someone likes your video, adds it to a playlist, or shares it, they are psychologically bonding with your channel. Design content that *invites* this — ask people to save it, share it, add it to a playlist. Each action deepens their loyalty.

---

### 2. 🥈 Emotional Attachment → Continued Donations
*β = 0.43 — strongest direct predictor of actually donating again*

How emotionally connected a viewer feels to *you as a person* — not just the content. This is a relationship, not a transaction. Viewers who feel genuinely attached to a creator keep donating regardless of external pressures.

**YouTube example:** Personal vlogs, behind-the-scenes content, sharing struggles and wins — anything that makes viewers feel they *know* you as a person rather than just consuming your output. This is what separates channels people subscribe to from channels people financially support.

---

### 3. 🥉 Participatory Interaction → Emotional Attachment
*γ = 0.46 — second strongest path overall*

Beyond building loyalty, liking and sharing content also builds personal emotional attachment. The act of publicly associating yourself with a creator's content makes you feel closer to them.

**YouTube example:** Engagement bait — asking viewers to like, comment, share — isn't just a cynical algorithm move. According to this research, every time someone acts on it, they genuinely feel more attached to you. It's a real psychological mechanism.

---

### 4. Emotional Loyalty → Continued Donations
*β = 0.34 — second direct predictor of donating again*

Loyalty built through repeated engagement translates into continued financial support. Loyal viewers don't get poached by other creators or stop donating when life gets busy — they're committed.

**YouTube example:** Consistency matters enormously here. Loyalty is built through repeated exposure and engagement over time — a reliable upload schedule and consistent style builds the kind of loyalty that survives dry spells.

---

### 5. Feedback Interaction → Emotional Attachment
*γ = 0.28 — positive but weaker than participatory interaction*

Direct two-way communication between creator and viewer — replies to comments, Q&As, DMs — does build emotional attachment, just less powerfully than passive engagement behaviours.

**YouTube example:** Replying to comments, doing community posts, responding to super chats — these matter and build genuine attachment, but don't exhaust yourself trying to reply to everything. The data suggests passive engagement tools (likes, shares) do more heavy lifting.

### ⚠️ One Important Warning

**Price consciousness *does* weaken emotional attachment's effect on donations** (β = −0.10) — meaning if a viewer is both emotionally attached *and* price-conscious, their attachment translates into less actual tipping. The practical lesson: **keep your entry-level tiers cheap.** Don't let price consciousness get in the way of an emotionally attached viewer acting on their feelings.

## 💡 The Big Takeaway

This study adds a crucial layer to everything else: **long-term tipping is about relationship, not rational decision-making.** Emotional attachment and loyalty — built through viewers engaging with and sharing your content — drive repeat donations more than anything else. The surprising finding is that *passive* engagement (likes, shares, reposts) builds loyalty more powerfully than actual conversation. Every viewer who shares your video is becoming more committed to you — whether they realise it or not.

## 📊 Factors Influencing Twitch Donation Decisions — Most to Least Important

**Important caveat first:** This study's overall model only explains **28% of donation variance** — meaning 72% of what drives donations on Twitch is unexplained by the seven factors tested. Take rankings with that in mind.

---

### 1. 🥇 Social Interaction — p = 0.011 *(only significant direct predictor)*

The **only factor** that directly and significantly predicts donation decisions. Viewers who feel they can interact — chat, ask questions, make friends with similar interests — are meaningfully more likely to donate.

**Twitch example:** Active chat rooms, responding to viewers during streams, building accessible community spaces outside Twitch (Discord, Facebook groups) all feed this directly.

---

### 2. 🥈 Enjoyment → Loyalty chain *(strongest indirect path)*

Enjoyment is the strongest predictor of loyalty (B = 0.698, R² = 0.563), and loyalty was *intended* to predict donations — though that final link surprisingly failed. Still, enjoyment → loyalty is the most powerful relationship in the entire model.

**Twitch example:** Entertainment value, reducing viewer loneliness and stress, and creating fun experiences build the deepest long-term loyalty.

---

### 3. 🥉 Personality *(strongest driver of both Community Building and Enjoyment)*

The streamer's visible personality — expressions, energy, gaming experience, on-screen presence — is the biggest shaper of both community building and enjoyment, making it the most upstream indirect influence on everything else.

**Twitch example:** On-camera charisma, genuine reactions, and consistent persona matter more than production quality for building the conditions that lead to donations.

---

### 4. Community Building → Loyalty *(significant indirect path)*

Community building significantly predicts loyalty (B = 0.271, p = 0.000), though again loyalty doesn't convert to donations as cleanly as hoped.

---

### ❌ What Doesn't Directly Drive Donations

| Factor | Result |
|---|---|
| **User engagement** | Not significant for donations directly |
| **Media richness** | Not significant |
| **Personality** | Not significant directly |
| **Community building** | Not significant directly |
| **Enjoyment** | Not significant directly |
| **Loyalty** | Surprisingly NOT significant (p = 0.185) — the final link in the chain breaks down |

## 💡 The Honest Takeaway

This is actually the **weakest and most cautionary** of the studies covered so far. The researchers built an elaborate seven-factor model and found that **only social interaction reliably predicts donations** — and even the full model leaves 72% of donation behaviour unexplained. The loyalty → donation link that the whole chain was building toward simply didn't hold. This suggests Twitch donations may be more spontaneous and in-the-moment than the other platforms studied, driven primarily by whether viewers feel socially connected *right now* during a stream.
