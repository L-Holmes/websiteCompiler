
**"THE CLIFF THAT SWALLOWED A WATERFALL"**

And rightly so as he wasn't the only one who took inspiration from this place. 


[
That's the thing about Malham Cove that gets me.

It doesn't look like Yorkshire. It looks like somewhere that shouldn't exist in England — a vast, curving amphitheatre of pale limestone rising out of a green valley, like someone forgot to finish building something enormous.

Wordsworth wrote a sonnet about it.

Harry Potter filmed here.

And somewhere underneath it, two rivers cross each other without mixing.

But none of that is the interesting bit.
] 

---

The interesting bit is what's missing.

Because this cliff — this entire curved 300-metre wall — was carved by something that isn't here anymore.

A waterfall.

Not a modest one.

At its peak, at the end of the last ice age, the cataract that thundered over this cliff was — by some accounts — twice the height of Niagara. Possibly the most spectacular waterfall in northern Europe.

[verify scale claim]

And then — over thousands of years — it simply disappeared.

The ice melted. The ground thawed. The water found the limestone. Limestone dissolves. The river went underground.

And left this.

An enormous, perfect, completely dry curved cliff. The shape of a waterfall with no water in it.

England's biggest waterfall is now Hardraw Force. Also in Yorkshire. 30 metres. [verify]

Malham was in a completely different category and there is nothing left of it except the shape it carved on the way out.

---

For most of its post-waterfall existence, the cove just stood here.

Farmers grazed below it. Tourists came and peered at it from the 1700s onwards — one visiting vicar in 1781 describing the occasional flood events as producing "a stupendous cataract, in height almost double that of Niagara."

Wordsworth came. Wrote his sonnet. Left.

Tolkien — if the story is right — stood at the base of it, looked up at that sheer curved wall of pale rock, and filed it away somewhere useful.

A decade or so later, a valley in Middle Earth needed defending.

---

Then in the 20th century, someone looked at this cliff and thought: I want to climb that.

Which, in retrospect, was inevitable.

Because Malham Cove is — for technical sport climbing — one of the finest walls in Europe. The limestone throws up sidepulls, undercuts, polished footholds, moves that are simultaneously delicate and brutally physical.

A local climber called Steve McClure has spent thirty years here. He's put up some of the hardest routes in British history on this wall.

In 2017, aged 46, after roughly 200 attempts, he finally climbed a route called Rainman. The first climb in Britain ever graded 9b — the hardest grade in the sport.

He fell off the last move the week before.

Came back. Did it.

Thirty years. One cliff. One man.

---

Word got out internationally.

In 2010, a 17-year-old Czech climber called Adam Ondra arrived in Yorkshire. Already considered the best sport climber alive, he turned up and casually did three of the hardest routes on the cove in a single day as a warm-up.

Then he tried McClure's hardest route.

Failed.

Went home.

Came back a year later. Spent seven days total across both trips before he finally got it. The best climber in the world, beaten repeatedly by a cliff in North Yorkshire, having to come back for a second go.

His description of Malham afterwards: "Such complex climbing — technical and super powerful at the same time."

High praise from someone who spends most of his time in Spain and France.

---

Above the climbers, every summer, peregrine falcons nest on the upper ledges.

The fastest animal on earth choosing the same cliff as the best climbers in the world.

Neither particularly bothered by the other.

---

On the 6th of December 2015, Storm Desmond arrived.

The rain was heavy enough that the underground cave system couldn't cope. The water backed up. Had nowhere to go.

And for a few hours, the waterfall came back.

80 metres. Straight down. 300 metres wide.

Briefly, England's highest single-drop waterfall — a title it technically holds about once every two centuries.

Two elderly locals said they'd never seen it. Their parents hadn't either.

People drove across the county to stand in the rain and watch it.

A few hours later, back underground. Gone.

The cove returned to exactly what it always is — dry, silent, enormous.

The shape of something that used to be there.

---

Tolkien had good taste.
