

On the 6th of December 2015, something happened in a Yorkshire valley that hadn't happened in nearly two hundred years.
A waterfall came back.
Not trickled. Not seeped. Genuinely poured — 80 metres straight down, 300 metres wide — for a few hours the highest single-drop waterfall in England.
Two elderly locals who'd lived their whole lives in the valley said they'd never seen it.
Then the water found its way back underground. And it was gone again.
Because here's the thing about Malham Cove.
There was a point in history when this was the most spectacular waterfall in England. Possibly in Europe.
Twice the height of Niagara. A curved amphitheatre of limestone, 300 metres wide, with a cataract thundering over the top of it.

That waterfall carved this cliff. And then, as the ice age ended and the ground thawed, the water found the cracks in the limestone — and simply went underground.
Leaving this enormous, silent, curved wall in a Yorkshire valley. Dry. Like nothing had ever happened.
England's biggest waterfall is now — for comparison — Hardraw Force in the same county. 30 metres. [verify]
Europe's biggest, Vinnufossen in Norway, is 860 metres.
Malham had something in that ballpark. And you can still see exactly where it was.
In 1781, a visiting vicar stood here and wrote that in floods it produced "a stupendous cataract, in height almost double that of Niagara."
By 1850, even those flood events had mostly stopped.
The cliff just stood here. Dry. Waiting.
Then the climbers found it.
Malham Cove turns out to be one of the finest sport climbing walls in Europe. The limestone is extraordinary — complex, technical, brutally physical in a way that attracts the best climbers in the world.
A man called Steve McClure has spent thirty years on this wall. In 2017, aged 46, after roughly 200 attempts, he climbed a route called Rainman. The first climb in Britain ever graded 9b — the hardest grade in the sport.
He fell off the last move the week before he finally did it.
In 2010, a 17-year-old called Adam Ondra arrived.
Already considered the best sport climber alive, he warmed up by doing three of the hardest routes on the cove in a single day. Then he tried McClure's hardest route at the time.
Failed.
Went home.
Came back the following year. Seven days of attempts across two visits. Foot slips. Forgotten sequences. One attempt where he was almost at the top and fell.
The best climber in the world. On a cliff in North Yorkshire. Twice.
His verdict: "Such complex climbing — technical and super powerful at the same time."
And above all of it, every summer, peregrine falcons nest on the upper ledges.
The fastest animal on earth, living on a dry waterfall in Yorkshire, while below them the best climbers in the world try and mostly fail to get to the top.
On the 6th of December 2015, Storm Desmond hit.
The underground cave system couldn't cope. The water had nowhere to go but over the top.
People drove across the county to watch it. Stood in the rain, seeing something their grandparents hadn't seen.
A few hours later it was gone. Back underground. Back through the limestone.
Leaving the cove exactly as it was.
Silent. Dry. Waiting for the next time.
