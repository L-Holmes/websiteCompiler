



“Why are there giant concrete cubes everywhere?”

### Intro

before time began, there was the cube

There’s a giant concrete box in a small English town.

It’s massive.  
It’s ugly.  
And it looks like it shouldn’t exist.

And for the longest time, I had absolutely no idea what it was.

But here’s the weird part—  
once you start noticing it… you start seeing them everywhere.

In cities.  
In deserts.  
Even in some of the most important places on Earth.

So the question is:

Why does the world keep building giant cubes?


### The Kendal Thing

This one’s in Kendal.

And yes—before you ask—  
did I once live in a house that overlooked this giant concrete monstrosity?

I can neither confirm nor deny.

Anyway.

What is it?

It’s a baby food factory.

That’s it.  
That’s the answer.

You can leave now.

…

But if you’re still here, you might be thinking:

Why is it *there*?  
Why does it look like *that*?  
And why does a baby food factory look like a rejected villain base from a low-budget sci-fi film?

Good questions.


### So… Why Is It There?

Short answer?

Beans.

Long answer—

Industrial buildings don’t care what they look like.

They care about:
- efficiency  
- cost  
- and doing one job *very* well  

And it turns out, if you want to store, process, or dry huge amounts of something…

The best shape is basically:

A box.

No wasted space.  
Easy to scale.  
Cheap to build.

Which is how you end up with a giant concrete cube in the middle of the countryside.


### It’s Not Just Kendal

Because once you notice this…

You realise Kendal isn’t special at all.

The world is full of these things.

There are massive, windowless boxes storing the internet.  
Entire warehouses the size of small towns.  
And buildings that look like they’re hiding something… because they basically are.

Like the AT&T Long Lines Building—  
a completely opaque skyscraper with almost no windows.

Or huge data centres in the middle of nowhere, quietly running modern life.

Same idea.

Different scale.

Still… just boxes.


### And Then It Gets Weird

Because cubes aren’t just practical.

They’re… everywhere.

One of the most important buildings in the world—  
the Kaaba—  
is, at its core, a cube.

There’s the Ka'ba-ye Zartosht,  
also… a cube.

Modern architects love them too.

Glass cubes.  
Black cubes.  
Reflective cubes.

At some point, we stopped just using boxes…

…and started *liking* them.


### The Realisation

And this is the weird part.

Cubes keep showing up because they solve problems:

They’re the simplest 3D shape to build.  
They use space efficiently.  
They scale easily.  
They’re structurally straightforward.

And maybe—  
for some reason—

Humans just trust them.

We’ve accidentally made the cube…

…the default shape of modern civilisation.


### A Quick Detour

There’s even a massive power station in South Africa called Kendal Power Station.

Which would be a perfect place to visit for this video.

If I had—

[cut to on-screen calculation]

Flights: £800  
Hotel: £400  
Taxi to a random power station: questionable life choices  

Total: absolutely not.


### Ending

So that giant concrete box in Kendal—

It’s not random.

It’s not unique.

It’s part of a pattern you can find all over the world.

A pattern of efficiency, simplicity…

…and a surprising number of cubes.

…

Still ugly though.
