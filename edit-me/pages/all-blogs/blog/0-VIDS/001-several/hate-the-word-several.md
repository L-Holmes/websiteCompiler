### Title Ideas: 
* The Word That Should Be Banned
* Why "Several" is the Worst Word in the English Language
* The 18th-Century Lie We All Believed

### Intro 

"beep. beep. beep. Hi errrmmm, I'm here to report that your Husband Jim has around aaaaa several percent chance of waking up"...
"errmehhh what!?"

I hate the word "several."

If I say I ate "several" cookies, how many are you picturing? I always thought seven. 
I mean.. just look at it.
But apparently, that’s wrong. 
But to find out why, we have to go back. Way back.


### The Backstory 

Several has been lingering around the dictionary ever since the 1400s, but after having a look into its history, 
I noticed that the use of the word "several" shoots up like a rocket ship about 200 years ago.
The culprit? A man named Thomas Paine.
In 1792, he released "Rights of Man". a book loudly cheering on the French Revolution..
Yeahh... It sounds like a something even a lawyer would fall asleep reading, and yet it sold like wildfire.
Basically the Harry Potter of its time (and thank god books have started to get better!)

It was even read aloud in taverns, which basically made it the 18th‑century version of a viral podcast.
And sure enough Paine uses “several” repeatedly—  I guess to sounddd smart??

Is it the only reason the word took off? Probably not. But there's no such thing as a coincidence.

### **Where Did It Come From (And Why Is It Broken)?**

But what does the word actually mean? Well, supposedly, it originates from the Latin _separalis_, meaning "separable."

So, really it’s closer to the word "separate" than "seven". 
You can see this in early American legal documents talking about the “several states.”
Which, unless I missed a massive historical plot twist, was definitely more than seven.

But then it went from meaning "distinct" to its current dictionary definition: "more than two but not very many." 

Which is... what? Three? Eight? Eleven? 
If someone says they saw 'Several Severuses', am I picturing three Alan Rickmans or seven?
Nobody knows!

But because so many want to sound 'authoritative', its been thrown around loads in legal documents,
which has lead to multiple hours of lawyers and politicians debating over it. 
Wasting lots of time... and worse, money.


### Why Extra Words Are Ruining Everything

But let’s play devil's advocate. Let's say you don't care about the "seven" confusion. 
You just love the thrill of the vagueness. 
Well, sorry to say but there is an even more fundamental problem here:
it is sort of a word that, really, isn't needed. And that's perhaps worse than you might think.

Any decent programmer will tell you that too much data can slow down and even break a system. 
It’s the same with our brains.
Every time someone reads the word "several," their brain has to pause, estimate, and guess. It costs processing time.
Milliseconds, sure. But multiply those milliseconds across every email, every news article, 
and every conversation on the planet, 
and its maybe its something we shouldn't overlook.

There’s a reason emergency signs just say "STOP"! 
..and not "Please bring your vehicle to a complete halt upon this here spot." 
A busy language slows us down. 
It inflates contracts, causes miscommunications, and quite literally costs money in data storage and server space.

and that's not the only thing- foreign language learners have it even worse!
It becomes a linguistic trap designed to out the people who aren't "in the know."
For readers with dyslexia or lower literacy, extra, useless words aren't just annoying; they prevent understanding.
"If a word doesn't add meaning, it only adds confusion."

### The Solution

But fear not, we have alternatives. "A few." "A handful." Or even just "some". 
These are brilliant because they actually do the job. If you can associate an image with a concept, it sticks.
"A handful" is perfect because you can actually see it.
Even if you'd never heard the word before, you'd know exactly what it meant.
That’s what a good word does—it communicates without the effort.

So why rely on a 200-year-old linguistic hangover? Tell someone you bought a "bucketload" of coffee. 
A shedload of books.. Or even a fridge‑load of cheese
[whispering] That you definitely didn't need...

Next time you feel the urge to use "several," spare a thought for little Jimmy in the back of the classroom,
 proudly holding up his drawing of "several" chickens.
 He’s blissfully unaware that his entire logical framework of the world
 is about to be dismantled by an 18th-century pamphlet writer.

And honestly? I think it’s time we take a stand.
Lets never utter this word again.
In fact, don’t even share this video. 
Let the algorithm bury it so deep that “several” becomes a forgotten relic of an unneeded history.
