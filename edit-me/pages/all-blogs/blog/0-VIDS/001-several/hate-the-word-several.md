### Intro 

If a surgeon says there’s a "several percent chance" you won't wake up, should you be worried? 
If your sat-nav says "turn left in several miles," where on earth are you? 
And if a judge orders you to pay "several damages," how much are you actually paying?

None of those make sense. And yet, that last one is a real legal ruling.

I hate the word "several."

Quick. Don't think. If I say I ate "several" cookies, how many are you picturing? I always thought seven. It is practically spelling it out for you. 
But apparently, that’s wrong. And looking into why it’s wrong uncovers a centuries-old trail of linguistic sabotage leading right to one of the key instigators of the American Revolution.
But to find out why, we have to go back. Way back.


### The Backstory 

Now, the word had actually been sitting quietly in the dictionary since the 1400s, bothering nobody. But if you look at the data, the use of "several" shoots up like a rocket ship about 200 years ago. The culprit? A man named Thomas Paine. In 1792, he released the book  "Rights of Man". Yeahh... It sounds like a something even a lawyer would fall asleep reading, and yet it sold 200,000 copies in a single year. To put that in perspective, that’s a thousand times bigger than a normal print run. Basically the Harry Potter of its time (and thank god books have started to get better!)

But here’s the thing: back then, 40% of the population couldn't actually read. That meant that small crowds would gather in taverns listen to the book being read aloud.It was the 18th-century version of a viral podcast. And I can only imagine the thrilling times they were having in those taverns: [Boring voice] 'The relative proportion of the foreign commerce of the several nations by which it can be distributed...' [Normal voice] yeah Wow. truly riveting. 
And guess what-- Paine used "several" constantly to sound authoritative and "smart".
Yeah.., I guess some things never change.

Is it the only reason the word took off? Maybe not. But there's no such thing as a coincidence.

### **Where Did It Come From (And Why Is It Broken)?**

But what does the word actually mean? Well, supposedly, it originates from the Latin _separalis_, meaning "separable."

So, etymologically speaking, it’s closer to the word "separate" than "seven". You can actually see this in early American legal documents, when they wrote about the "several states". They literally meant the _separate_, individual states. 

But somehow, it changed. It went from meaning "distinct" to its current dictionary definition: "more than two but not very many." 

Which is... what? Three? Eight? Eleven? 
If someone says they saw 'Several Severuses', am I picturing three Alan Rickmans or seven?
Nobody knows!



Because nobody can agree on what it means, this single word has cost lawyers hundreds of billable hours. Just look at the legal system. In 1952, the UK House of Lords literally had to debate removing the word "several" from the Intestates' Estates Bill because they realised it was entirely useless. In 1972, a Maryland court threw their hands up and tossed a ruling because "several" was deemed far too non-specific to enforce. And as recently as 2021, a Minnesota Supreme Court case hinged—in part—on trying to figure out what "several" actually means.

It is a word so fundamentally broken that supreme courts and parliaments have had to officially, legally ignore it.


### Why Extra Words Are Ruining Everything

But let’s play devil's advocate. Let's say you don't care about the "seven" confusion. You just love the thrill of the vagueness. Well, sorry to say but there is an even more fundamental problem here:
it is sort of a word that, really, isn't needed. And that's perhaps worse than you might think.

Any decent programmer will tell you that too much data can slow down and even break a system. 
It’s the same with our brains.
Every time someone reads the word "several," their brain has to pause, estimate, and guess. It costs processing time. Milliseconds, sure. But multiply those milliseconds across every email, every news article, and every conversation on the planet, and its maybe its something we shouldn't overlook.

There’s a reason emergency signs just say "STOP" and not "Please bring your vehicle to a complete halt upon this here spot." 
A busy language slows humanity down. It inflates contracts, causes miscommunications, and quite literally costs money in data storage and server space.

And foreign language learners have it even worse!
It becomes a linguistic trap designed to out the people who aren't "in the know." <show 'Shibboleth—' on screen>
For readers with dyslexia or lower literacy, extra, useless words aren't just annoying; they are a direct barrier to understanding. As the great, entirely made-up philosopher Thesaurio once said: "If a word doesn't add meaning, it only adds confusion."

### The Solution


But fear not, we have alternatives. "A few." "A handful." Or even just "some". These are brilliant because they actually do the job. If you can associate an image with a concept, it sticks. "A handful" is perfect because you can actually see it. Even if you'd never heard the word before, you'd know exactly what it meant. That’s what a good word does—it communicates without the effort.

So why rely on a 200-year-old linguistic hangover? Tell someone you bought a "bucketload" of coffee. A shedload of books.. Or even a "totebag-load" of rocks. 

Next time you feel the urge to use "several," spare a thought for little Jimmy in the back of the classroom, proudly holding up his drawing of "several" chickens. He’s blissfully unaware that his entire logical framework of the world is about to be dismantled by an 18th-century pamphlet writer.

Let's fight back. Never utter this word again. In fact, don't even share this video. Let the algorithm bury it until the very concept of the word is lost to history. Let’s defeat it, once and for all.
