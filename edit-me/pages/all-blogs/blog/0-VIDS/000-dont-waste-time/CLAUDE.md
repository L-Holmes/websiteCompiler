**"YOU'RE WASTING YOUR INTELLIGENCE"**

---

There's a physicist I used to know.

Genuinely brilliant. Could tell you exactly why the sky is blue, why ice floats, why time slows down near a gravitational mass.

He spent four hours a day on a football statistics forum.

Not playing or coaching. Just... accumulating facts about matches that had already happened, that nobody was paying him to know, that changed nothing about his life or anyone else's.

Brilliant mind. Completely misdirected.

---

Here's something that's been bothering me.

The smartest people of this generation — genuinely, measurably smart — are being quietly recruited to solve one problem:

*How do we get people to keep scrolling?*

That's it. That's the brief.

The engineers at Meta who built the recommendation algorithm are not stupid people. Some of them are exceptional. And they spent years of their lives making it slightly harder for you to put your phone down.

[verify — there are documented accounts from former Facebook engineers on this]

That's where the intelligence went.

# ---

I want to talk about memory for a second.

There's a meta-study from 2014 that found screen time in childhood doesn't just displace activity — it displaces the *formation of memories.* [verify — find the specific study]

The summers you remember aren't the ones where you watched the most TV.

They're the ones where something *happened.*

I realised this properly at the end of my second year at university. I tried to think about what I actually remembered from the year. Lectures, a few nights out, some anxiety about deadlines.

That was basically it. A year. Gone.

Not because nothing happened. Because I hadn't been paying attention in the right direction.

---

I'm not going to pretend I've always been good at this.

I wasn't.

Games, mostly. Hours of them. And look — I have warm memories attached to some of that. Friends, late nights, the particular feeling of being fifteen and having nowhere to be.

But there's a difference between that and what it became later. Sitting alone. Getting good at something that doesn't transfer. Building skills inside a system specifically designed to keep you inside it.

The nostalgia is real. But The value of the time spent doing those things is a different question.

---

I made a website once. Spent weeks on it.

Then a tool for homework. Then a custom keyboard layout that maybe three people on earth needed.

None of it went anywhere. Not because I'm not capable — I think I am, most days — but because I was applying effort to things that couldn't compound. Things that didn't distinguish me, didn't further anything, didn't lead anywhere the next thing could build on.

The blogs I wrote that actually got traction? It wasn't the design. It wasn't the technical execution. It was the *idea.* The specific angle that nobody else had taken.

That's the thing that's hard to copy. That's the thing worth spending time on.

---

Prioritisation sounds boring. It isn't.

It's just the question: what is the actual goal?

And I'd push back gently on some of the obvious answers.

Money — okay, but money for what?
A first-class degree — fine, but then what?
A family — genuinely admirable, but is that *your* answer or the one that was handed to you?

I know families that look, from the outside, like the goal everyone's supposed to be aiming at. And some of them are quietly miserable.

The honest answer — the one that doesn't move when you press on it — is something like: a life that feels worth living. Content. Moving. Occasionally surprised by itself.

Everything else is a proxy for that.

---

I started this channel.

It might not go anywhere. I genuinely don't know.

But it's already done something — it's pushed me down a path, and that path has branches, and some of those branches lead somewhere interesting. I can feel it even if I can't see it yet.

Sitting in a dark room getting better at a game that forgets your progress when the servers go down doesn't do that.

---

If you're watching this in the middle of the day —

genuinely — close the tab.

Go and make something. Anything. Something that exists after you stop working on it.

It doesn't have to be good. It just has to be *yours.*

---

I don't regret where I am.

I'd give the same advice to anyone, and I'm aware that's easy to say from where I'm standing. I was fortunate — not bogged down by the things that bog a lot of people down — and I'm grateful for that every day.

But the knowledge was there, earlier. It just wasn't pointed at anything.

That's the bit I'd change.
