
In 2007, a 23-year-old called Eduardo sat in a deposition room in Manhattan.

Lawyers. Suits. The works.

He'd just been squeezed out of a company he helped build. The settlement was, by most accounts, somewhere north of a billion dollars.

He was 23.

The company was Facebook.

The lawyers asked him, at one point, what he thought the platform would become.

He said he wasn't sure it would become anything.

Here's what I keep thinking about.

The people who built the attention economy didn't build it because they wanted to waste your time.

They built it because they were smart, and solving hard problems felt good, and the hardest problem in front of them was: *how do we keep people here longer?*

That's an interesting engineering challenge. Genuinely.

And they solved it. Spectacularly.

The average person now touches their phone over **2,000 times a day.** [verify — Dscout research or similar]

They solved the problem completely. They just maybe picked the wrong problem.

There's a name for this in chess.

A "brilliancy" — a move so clever, so elegant, so unexpected — that the crowd gasps.

Grandmasters lose games on brilliancies. They get so absorbed in the beauty of the solution that they forget to ask whether the solution is actually *useful.*

Smart people do this constantly.

Not in chess. In life.

---

I did it too.

I spent — and I'm going to be honest about this — probably 18 months of evenings building things that went nowhere.

A website. A homework tool. A custom keyboard layout that I was genuinely convinced was going to change how I worked.

The keyboard layout took three weeks. 

Three weeks.

For what? an insignificant change in my writst RSI?

I was solving the wrong problem with everything I had.

The uncomfortable thing about intelligence is that it doesn't come with a compass.

It just makes you *faster.* Better at learning, better at optimising, better at building.

But faster in which direction?

I once knew a physicist I came across — [find real example] — spent years becoming one of the most knowledgeable people alive on the subject of Premier League statistics. 
Not professionally. Just... because the problem was there and his brain wanted something to grip onto.

He wasn't lazy. He was the opposite of lazy.

He was just aimed at the wrong thing.

---

Here's the question I'd ask my younger self — and I mean this specifically, not as a motivational poster:

*What are you building that will still exist in five years?*

Not "what are you getting better at." Not "what are you enjoying."

What exists, after you stop, that wouldn't have existed without you?

A game save doesn't count. A high score doesn't count. An encyclopaedic knowledge of a fictional universe's lore — genuinely, lovingly acquired — doesn't count.

Something that compounds. Something that someone else can find.

---

The blogs I wrote that actually worked — that got read, that led somewhere — weren't technically impressive.

The design was fine. The SEO was fine.

It was the *angle.* The specific, slightly strange take that nobody else had bothered to write down.

That's the thing that's hard to replicate. That's where the intelligence should have been going the whole time.

Not "how do I build this well."

"What is worth building."

---

There's a version of this that gets said badly — the "put down your phone and go outside" version — and I want to be careful not to say that version.

Because the problem isn't screens. It's *direction.*

The internet is extraordinary. The tools available to a curious person right now are genuinely unprecedented.

You can learn almost anything. Build almost anything. Reach almost anyone.

The question is what you're doing with that.

Consuming, mostly. Which is fine, up to a point.

But consumption doesn't distinguish you. It doesn't compound. It doesn't lead.

---

I started this channel knowing it might go nowhere.

It might. That's a real possibility.

But even if it does — it's made me think harder, write more carefully, notice things I'd have scrolled past.

It's a path. Paths have branches. Branches lead somewhere, eventually, if you keep walking.

The dark room doesn't have branches.

---

You already have what you need.

That's the bit that's genuinely frustrating to say, because it sounds like a greeting card.

But the knowledge is there. The capability is there. It was probably there years ago.

It's just been going in the wrong direction.

Point it somewhere that matters.

Then see what happens.
