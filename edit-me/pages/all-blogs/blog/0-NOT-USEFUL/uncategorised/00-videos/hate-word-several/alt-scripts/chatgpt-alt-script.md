

# The Word That Should Be Banned

## Intro (Hook)

There’s a word in English that has probably caused **millions of tiny misunderstandings**…

…has shown up in **actual court cases**…

…and there’s a decent chance you don’t fully agree on what it means with your own family.

That word is:

*several.*

Quick test.

If I say:
“I ate several biscuits.”

What number are you picturing?

Be honest.

Because if you thought “seven”…
you’re not alone.

---

## The Problem (Fast)

Here’s the issue:

* Ask 100 people what “several” means
* You will not get one answer
* You won’t even get *close*

You’ll get:

* 3
* 4–6
* 7 (the enlightened few)
* “More than a couple”
* “I don’t know, just… some”

That’s not a word.

That’s a **vibe**.

And vibes are not useful when you’re trying to communicate actual information.

---

## Why This Actually Matters

This isn’t just pedantic nonsense.

This word has caused:

* **Legal ambiguity** — courts arguing over what “several” even means
* **Contract disputes** — because “several days” could mean 3… or 10
* **Everyday confusion** — constantly, quietly, forever

There are cases where judges have basically had to go:

> “Right… we’re going to ignore the word ‘several’ because it’s useless.”

That’s not me being dramatic.

That’s the legal system giving up.

---

## The Explosion (Stats, Not Storytime)

Now here’s where it gets interesting.

The word *several* didn’t just casually exist.

It **exploded** in usage around the early 1800s.

* Around **1814** → peak usage
* One book in 1792 → **200,000 copies**
* Normal books at the time → **~1,000 copies**

That’s not a bestseller.

That’s a **100x–1000x anomaly**.

And because literacy rates were low—around **40%+ couldn’t read**—

those books weren’t just read…

They were performed.

Out loud.
In taverns.
To groups.

Meaning one copy could reach **dozens of people at once**.

So a single author’s word choices didn’t just spread…

They **echoed**.

---

## The Villain (Light Drama)

And right in the middle of this…

You’ve got Thomas Paine.

Trying to sound clever.
Trying to sound precise.
Trying to sound… important.

And instead, accidentally helping popularise one of the most useless words in the English language.

Now—to be fair—it wasn’t just him.

But when you’re pushing numbers like that?

You don’t need to invent the word.

You just need to **amplify it**.

---

## The Twist (Origin)

Because here’s the twist:

*several* has **nothing to do with seven.**

At all.

It comes from a word meaning:

“separate”

As in:

* “several states” → *individual states*
* not “a mystery number of states”

So originally, it actually made sense.

Then over time, it drifted into:

“some unspecified number”

Which is linguistic for:

“I don’t want to commit to a number.”

---

## The Real Problem

This is the core issue:

**It pretends to be precise.**

But it isn’t.

Compare:

* “three” → clear
* “about five” → still useful
* “a handful” → visual, intuitive
* “several” → ???

It sounds formal.

It sounds educated.

It sounds like you know what you’re talking about.

But you don’t.

You’ve just outsourced the thinking to whoever’s listening.

---

## Why That’s Bad (Quick Hits)

Every time you use a vague word like this:

* The listener has to guess
* The brain does extra work
* Misunderstandings increase

Scale that up across millions of conversations?

That’s a lot of wasted time.

A lot of friction.

All caused by a word that doesn’t need to exist.

---

## Better Options (Snappy)

We already solved this problem:

* “A few”
* “A handful”
* “Around five”
* “Less than ten”

These all:

* communicate something real
* reduce ambiguity
* don’t start philosophical debates mid-conversation

Or go fun with it:

* “a bucketload”
* “a truckload”

At least those admit they’re being vague.

*Several* pretends it isn’t.

## Emotional Punch (Jimmy, But Faster)

Somewhere out there…

there is a kid who heard “several”
and confidently thought it meant seven.

And one day—

they got corrected.

Publicly.

That moment sticks.

Not because they were wrong…

…but because the word is broken.

---

## Ending (Call to Action)

So here’s the plan:

We retire it.

No announcements.
No ceremony.

We just… stop.

Don’t use it.
Don’t defend it.
Don’t pass it on.

Let it quietly disappear.

Because if a word:

* confuses people
* adds no real meaning
* and even courts don’t trust it…

Then maybe—

it shouldn’t exist at all.
