**[Visual: Google Ngram Viewer chart popping up. A massive, undeniable spike starts around the late 1700s and peaks squarely around 1814.]**

**Voiceover:** If you look at the historical data, the use of the word "several" shoots up like a rocket ship right at the end of the 18th century. And a massive culprit for this sudden linguistic infection? A man named Thomas Paine. 

**[Visual: A slightly mocking, dramatic portrait of Thomas Paine with a quill.]**

**Voiceover:** In 1792, Paine released a book with the less-than-thrilling title, _Rights of Man_. Despite sounding like required reading for a law degree, it sold astronomically. **[Visual: The word SEVERAL on a chalkboard. A chalk line strikes through the 'AL' and replaces it with 'SEVER'.]**

**Voiceover:** But what does it actually mean? Well, supposedly, it originates from the Latin _separalis_, meaning "separable." Or, the verb "to sever."

When they wrote about the "several states," they didn't mean a random handful of states. They literally meant the _separate_, individual states.

**Voiceover:** And this shift from "separate" to "an indistinct, vague number somewhere between three and six" has caused absolute chaos. Just look at the legal system. Even if you've never heard the phrase before, you can deduce what it means.
