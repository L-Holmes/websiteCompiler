# THE WORD THAT SHOULDN'T EXIST

---

**[HOOK]**

A surgeon tells you the operation has "a several percent chance of going wrong."

A judge rules that "several damages must be paid."

Your sat nav says "turn left in several miles."

Useless. All of them. And yet one of those is real. One of those has actually ended up in court. Twice.

The word "several." It is broken. It has always been broken. And one man, two hundred years ago, accidentally broke it for everyone.

---

**[THE CONFUSION]**

Quick. Don't think. How many is "several"?

Most people say seven. Or somewhere around seven. And honestly? That's not stupid. "Sev-eral." "Sev-en." The resemblance isn't a coincidence — they share a Latin root.

But the dictionary? The dictionary says several means "more than two but not very many." Which is... three? Eight? Eleven? Nobody knows. There is no agreed number. It is legally, mathematically, and practically meaningless.

In 1972, a Maryland court threw out a ruling partly because the word "several" was too vague to enforce.
In 2021, a Minnesota Supreme Court case turned — in part — on what "several" actually means.

A single word. Costing lawyers hundreds of billable hours. Because nobody can agree what it means.

---

**[THE VILLAIN]**

1792. Thomas Paine publishes *Rights of Man.*

This isn't a niche academic pamphlet. By 1793 — one year later — an estimated 200,000 copies are in circulation. For context: a normal book print run at the time was 750 to 1,250 copies. Paine's book outsold the average by up to a thousand times over.

And here's the thing about 1790s England — 40% of the population couldn't read. So these books weren't just being read privately. They were being read *aloud*. In taverns. To crowds. The word didn't just spread through literacy. It spread through speech, through pubs, through word of mouth in the most literal sense possible.

Paine used "several" constantly. And the word peaked — right there — around 1814.

One man. One book. Half a nation's vocabulary, permanently bent.

---

**[WHY THIS ACTUALLY MATTERS]**

"It's just a word." Sure. But language is a compression format for thought. Every redundant word is dead weight in the system.

The brain has finite memory. We don't know exactly how much — but we know it's not infinite. A word that means nothing precise isn't neutral. It takes up space. It costs processing time. Every time someone reads "several," their brain has to pause, estimate, and guess. Milliseconds, yes. But multiplied across every email, every article, every conversation — it adds up.

Foreign language learners have it worse. English already has one of the largest vocabularies in the world — over 170,000 words in active use — largely because it cannibalised French, Latin, Norse, and German for centuries. Every redundant synonym is another thing to memorise, another trap to fall into.

And in the digital world? Longer, vaguer words compress worse. They index worse. If you search for "several," you get chaos. The same concept, scattered across a thousand different phrasings, none of them findable.

---

**[THE BETTER OPTIONS]**

"A few." "A handful." Both do the job. Both are honest about being vague.

"A handful" is actually brilliant — you can picture it. You've never met the word before and you already know roughly what it means. That's what a good word does. It communicates without effort.

Meanwhile "several" pretends to be specific. It sounds like a number. It has the shape of precision without any of the substance. That's almost worse than just saying "some."

---

**[THE CLOSER]**

Somewhere right now, a kid is drawing "several" chickens for a homework assignment.

He's drawn seven. Obviously. Because *sev*eral. Because that's what your brain does with that word.

And his teacher is about to mark it wrong.

Not because he can't count. Not because he doesn't understand the task. But because two hundred years ago, a very popular man used a broken word in a very popular book, and nobody ever stopped to ask whether we actually needed it.

We didn't. We still don't.

So next time it comes up — just say "a few." Say "a handful." Say literally any number.

Just don't say several.
