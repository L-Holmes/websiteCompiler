### The Backstory 


If you look at the historical data, the use of the word "several" shoots up like a rocket ship around 200 years ago. And a massive culprit for this sudden jump? A man named Thomas Paine.  <shivers>


In 1792, Paine released a book with the ever-so-thrilling title, _Rights of Man_. Despite sounding like required reading for a law degree, it sold astronomically. We’re talking 200,000 copies in circulation by 1793. To put that in perspective, Paine’s work was literally up to a thousand times bigger than a normal book. 
[That's like the equivalent of [harry potter] in the modern day... Thank god we've got better books now!]

But also! Don't forget, back in 18th century England, over forty per cent of the population was illiterate. So what did they do? They read this book aloud in taverns. Given the clear lack of alternative entertainment in the 1790s, the words in this book spread like wildfire. Paine, trying to sound smart and authoritative, alongside other writers of the era, cemented "several" into the public consciousness. 
Hmmm.. people using a new confusing word to sound smart... does that sound familiar?

### Where Did It Come From?


But what does it actually mean? Well, supposedly, it originates from the Latin _separalis_, meaning "separable." Or, the verb "to sever." 


So, etymologically speaking, it’s closer to the word "separate" than "seven." You can actually see this in early American legal documents. When they wrote about the "several states," they didn't mean a random handful of states. They literally meant the _separate_, individual states. 


And this shift from "separate" to "an indistinct, vague number somewhere between three and six" has caused absolute chaos. Just look at the legal system. In 1972, a Maryland court had to throw their hands up because "several" was deemed far too non-specific for a legal ruling. In 1952, the UK House of Lords literally had to debate removing the word "several" from the Intestates' Estates Bill because it was entirely useless. It is a word so bad that supreme courts and parliaments have had to officially legally ignore it.

### Why Extra Words Are Ruining Everything


But let’s play devil's advocate. Let's say you don't care about the "seven" confusion. Let's say you get invigorated by the vagueness. There is a much bigger, much more fundamental problem here. 
It is an entirely wasted word.

Sometimes, extra words are fine. Variety is the spice of life, as they say. But as any decent programmer will tell you, we don't need redundant data clogging up the system. Why? Because it wastes brain space. 

We don't know exactly how much memory a human brain has, but we know that its not infinite. Imagine a library. Every single additional book you shove into that library makes it marginally harder to find the exact book you're actually looking for. It’s the same in computer RAM, and it’s the same in your head. It might be a minuscule amount of wasted space, but it adds up. 


Every extra word takes up mental space. It costs processing time. Every time someone reads "several," their brain has to pause, estimate, and guess. Milliseconds, yes. But multiplied across every email, every article, every conversation — it adds up.
 There’s a reason emergency signs just say "STOP" and not "Please bring your vehicle to a complete halt upon this here spot." Bloated language slows us down. It makes text messages drag, it inflates legal contracts, and it costs literal money in data storage, compression, and server space. 


Foreign language learners have it worse. 
It becomes a Shibboleth—a linguistic trap designed to out the people who aren't "in the know." 
For readers with dyslexia or lower literacy, extra, useless words aren't just annoying; they are a direct barrier to understanding. As the great, entirely made-up philosopher Thesaurio once said: "If a word doesn't add meaning, it only adds confusion."

### The Solution


But, fear not. We have alternatives. "A few." "A handful." These are brilliant because they actually do the job, and they do it better. 
Education psychology tells us that if you can associate an image with a concept, it sticks. 
"A handful" is perfect. 
"A handful" is actually perfect. 
You can picture it. 
You've never met the word before and you already know roughly what it means. That's what a good word does. It communicates without effort.

So why not expand on that? Why rely on a 200-year-old hangover of a word? Next time, tell someone you saw a "bucketload" of coffee. A "totebag load" of gogos crazy bones. A "bookbag load" of... well books.
