# The UK has a problem with motorway exit names. Giraffe Junction is the solution.

---

## THE PROBLEM

Numbers work. Until they don't.

And on a motorway — where you're doing 70, you've got about four seconds to read a sign, and the junction you want is coming up in... actually, is it this one or the next one?

That's the moment. That's when people pick up their phones.

Not to call someone. Not to text.
Just to figure out where they are.

And honestly? I don't blame them.
Because the system has completely failed them at that point.

---

## THE CLOSE JUNCTIONS PROBLEM

Here's something nobody talks about: junction spacing.

On some stretches of motorway, junctions come every mile or two.
M25 around London — some gaps are barely 90 seconds apart at motorway speed.

So you're doing 70. You see a sign. Junction 12.
Is that yours? You're not sure. You think it might be 13.
You hesitate. You miss it.

Now you're doing an extra 8 miles to the next roundabout and back.

If that junction had a *name* — something you'd looked up before you left —
you wouldn't have hesitated.
You'd have known.

---

## THE PSYCHOLOGY BIT

Your brain is genuinely bad at numbers.

Not because you're dim — because that's just how memory works.

Numbers are abstract. They have no image. No texture. Nothing to grab onto.

But give something a *name* — a real name, something with a picture attached —
and suddenly your brain has somewhere to put it.

More associations → more connections → easier to recall at 70mph in the rain
when you've got a lorry on your left and a Peugeot cutting across your lane.

This is why "Spaghetti Junction" works.

Nobody calls it Junction 6 of the M6. Nobody.
A journalist at the Birmingham Mail looked at the plans in 1965, said it looked like
"a cross between a plate of spaghetti and an unsuccessful attempt at a Staffordshire knot" —
and that name has outlived every road sign that's ever been near it.

It's in the Oxford English Dictionary.
It appears in films.
It's on vestments in Birmingham Cathedral.

Junction 6 could never.

---

## WHAT OTHER COUNTRIES DID

Now — here's where it gets interesting.

Germany. The Autobahn. Famous for no speed limits, engineering precision, efficiency.

They number their motorways, same as us.
But where two motorways *meet* — where you actually need to make a decision —
they name it.

*Kamener Kreuz.* The Kamen Cross. Near Dortmund.
*Frankfurter Kreuz.* Frankfurt Junction. One of the busiest in Europe.
*Hermsdorfer Kreuz. Maschener Kreuz. Horster Dreieck.*

These are *places.* People know them. Drivers build mental maps around them.

"Oh yeah, take the A1, come off at Kamener Kreuz."

That sentence makes spatial sense in a way that "come off at junction 37" simply doesn't.

Now — the UK did accidentally stumble into one famous example.
But it took a journalist's throwaway metaphor in 1965 to do it.

What if we just... *did it on purpose?*

---

## THE FUN ARGUMENT (which is actually the serious argument)

Forget safety for a second.

Doesn't the current system just seem a bit... bleak?

We named our pubs. We named our fields.
We have villages called Giggleswick, Pity Me, and Nether Wallop.

But the thing millions of people use every single day?
Junction 14. Junction 15. Junction 16.

I would genuinely drive further just to find out what the junction names were.

I'd be doing a *junction challenge* — selfie in front of the sign,
like a parkrunner chasing a new 5K every Saturday morning.
Uploading to the subreddit. Arguing about the best ones.

And look — there are already people in this country photographing every lighthouse,
every trig point, every Ordnance Survey benchmark.

Junction tourism is *one memorable name* away from being a legitimate hobby.

(Maybe that's exactly why nobody's done it.
Can you imagine the Highways England inbox.)

---

## THE SOLUTION

It doesn't have to be complicated.

Local landmark. Local geography. Local history.
Something that makes a driver, on their first time doing this route,
*picture* where they're going before they even get there.

Something a lorry driver could say on the phone —
"Yeah, come off at [NAME], you can't miss it."

And actually mean it.

---

## OUTRO

The name Giraffe Junction doesn't exist yet.

But it should.

And if you've ever missed a junction because you had no idea which number you were on —
if you've ever reached for your phone on a motorway just to orient yourself —
then you already know I'm right.

Name the junctions.
Make the roads make sense.

And in the meantime — eyes on the road.

You've got no idea where Junction 37 is.
