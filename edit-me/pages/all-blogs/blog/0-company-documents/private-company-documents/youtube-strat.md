# CLAUDE REQUEST!!!

**YOUTUBE SCRIPT GENERATION PROMPT**

---

Write me a YouTube video script. Here is everything you need to know about the style, tone, and format:

**THE VOICE**

The presenter is British, conversational, and slightly self-aware. Not a lecturer. Not a motivational speaker. Someone smart talking to someone else smart, who might leave at any moment and knows it. The tone is warm but unsentimental. Honest without being preachy. Occasionally dry. Never smug.

Think CGP Grey, Tom Scott, Not Just Bikes — that register. Fast-moving. Trusts the audience. Doesn't over-explain.

Avoid anything that sounds like:
- An AI wrote it
- An American lifestyle guru wrote it
- A GCSE presentation wrote it

**STRUCTURE**

No formal intro. No "hey guys, welcome back." Start immediately — mid-story, mid-statistic, mid-scene. The viewer should feel like they've walked into something already happening.

From there, the script should move in short bursts. Each section earns the next one. No section should outstay its welcome.

End with something personal and quiet. Not a rallying cry. Not a call to action dressed up as wisdom. Something that lands and then stops.

**HOOKS — THESE ARE NON-NEGOTIABLE**

Every 60-90 seconds there needs to be a reason to keep watching. These hooks should be:
- A specific person and what happened to them
- A statistic that reframes something familiar
- A question that doesn't have an obvious answer
- A personal admission that feels genuinely honest rather than performed

Avoid: vague emotional appeals, rhetorical questions that answer themselves, anything that could appear on a motivational Instagram account.

**LANGUAGE**

Short sentences. Fragments are fine. Emphasis through rhythm, not through bold text or exclamation marks.

Never use:
- "It's important to note that..."
- "In today's world..."
- "What if I told you..."
- "Let that sink in."
- Any variation of "join me on this journey"

British spellings throughout.

**STORIES**

Every abstract point needs a specific story attached to it. Real people, real situations, real details. If a real example doesn't exist, write a plausible illustrative one and mark it clearly in the notes with [find real version of this].

The story comes *before* the point, not after. Show, then tell. Never the other way round.

**STATISTICS**

Use them. They should feel surprising, specific, and slightly uncomfortable. Not reassuring.

Every statistic that needs verification should be left in the script with a square bracket note, e.g. [verify — WHO / NHS / specific study]. Do not remove these. Do not smooth over uncertainty. Leave the research flags visible.

**FORMAT**

- No scene directions, no camera notes, no visual suggestions — just the spoken words
- No headers or section titles in the final script
- Paragraph breaks represent natural pauses or shifts
- Italics only for genuine spoken emphasis, used sparingly
- Leave [square bracket research notes] wherever a fact needs verifying or a story needs finding
- End with a clearly separated NOTES section listing everything that needs to be checked, sourced, or reconsidered before filming

**WHAT TO AVOID**

- Moralising. Make the argument, trust the audience to draw the conclusion.
- Repetition of the same point in different words — if it's been said, move on
- Anything that sounds like a pep talk
- Passive aggression dressed up as concern
- The word "journey"
- Ending on a question

**THE TEST**

Before finishing, read it back and ask:
- Does it start without warming up?
- Does every section earn the next?
- Is there a real human being in this, not just a perspective?
- Would someone who doesn't care about this topic stay anyway?

If any of those answers is no, cut or rewrite until they are.
