    How we measure The criteria we use to compare ourselves against alternatives in the table below up

    • 📦 Information density What % of words are directly actionable? Relative to the most concise source (us = 100%). e.g. Huberman ≈ 30%; Chris Williamson ≈ 1%
    • 🗂️ Topics covered Raw count of distinct life-improvement topics addressed. e.g. Us = 120+; Huberman ≈ 30
    • 🎯 % topics relevant to a better life Of topics covered, how many actually apply to a general person trying to reduce day-to-day negativity? e.g. Us = 100%; Huberman ≈ 80%
    • ⚡ % topics that are actionable Of relevant topics, how many give you something you can actually do today? e.g. Us = 100%; Huberman ≈ 40% (still very good!)
    • ✅ Accuracy (% not false / misleading) e.g. Roka glasses, unnecessary supplements when no deficiency exists, probiotic hype... We aim for 100% by flagging uncertainty rather than presenting guesses as fact
    • ⏱️ Time to find all advice How long would it take a new reader to locate every single actionable strategy? Us ≈ 5 mins; Huberman ≈ 500 hours
    • 💰 Cost consideration Does every recommendation weigh up the financial cost vs the predicted negativity reduction? Money buys freedom from sadness — ignoring it is a mistake
    • ⚖️ Impact weighting Is each item ranked by how much it will actually move the needle for most people? So you can prioritise and skip low-ROI changes
    • 🔬 Reliability / safety evaluation Science is fallible — conflicting diet studies are a perfect example. We ground recommendations in ancestral/paleo principles and flag uncertainty levels, rather than treating all peer-reviewed papers as equal
